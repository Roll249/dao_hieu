% ABSTRACT
%=============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
The convergence of sixth-generation (6G) wireless networks and Vehicular Edge Computing (VEC) has given rise to heterogeneous Terrestrial--Non-Terrestrial Network (T-NTN) architectures in which latency-sensitive tasks must be offloaded across Road-Side Units (RSUs), Low-Altitude Platforms (LAPs), High-Altitude Platforms (HAPs), and Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites under strict delay and energy constraints. Existing Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) approaches address this problem with multiple sequential Deep Q-Networks (DQNs) \cite{hrl_ntn}, yet this design introduces prohibitive parameter overhead, slow inter-layer coordination, and poor scalability when the action space grows with network size.
To overcome these limitations, we propose \textbf{Quantum-HRL}, a hybrid quantum-classical framework that replaces the multi-DQN pipeline with two complementary quantum subroutines. First, a Variational Quantum Circuit (VQC) \cite{vqc_drl} encodes the environment state via Amplitude Encoding into $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits and simultaneously produces the high-level decisions of layer selection and offloading ratio $\alpha$, trained via the Parameter-Shift Rule. Second, the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) \cite{farhi2014quantum} solves the resulting node-selection task as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem, yielding the optimal edge node in a single measurement. Bayesian Optimization \cite{bo_qaoa} serves as a noise-robust classical subroutine to tune the QAOA angles with minimal quantum circuit evaluations, enabling deployment on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices~\cite{preskill2018nisq}.
Simulation over five independent random seeds demonstrates that Quantum-HRL achieves $20.3\%$ lower mean end-to-end task latency than the classical HRL baseline \cite{hrl_ntn} ($0.117\pm0.038$\,s vs.\ $0.147\pm0.016$\,s; Welch's $t$-test and Mann--Whitney $U$, both $p<0.001$) with zero deadline violations, while reducing the number of trainable parameters by $99.9\%$ (24 vs.\ 20{,}224, an $843\times$ compression). The latency gain is obtained under an explicitly quantified latency--energy trade-off ($0.97$\,J vs.\ $0.35$\,J per task), which we analyse in detail---confirming that quantum-enhanced policy networks can deliver latency and scalability benefits with a drastically smaller model footprint for next-generation vehicular edge computing.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Task offloading; non-terrestrial networks; hierarchical reinforcement learning; variational quantum circuits; QAOA; vehicular edge computing; NISQ.

% I. INTRODUCTION
%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The proliferation of connected and autonomous vehicles is driving an unprecedented surge in latency-sensitive computation tasks---object detection, path planning, V2X collision avoidance---that far exceed the onboard processing capacity of individual vehicles. Emerging sixth-generation (6G) networks respond by deploying \emph{Terrestrial--Non-Terrestrial Network} (T-NTN) architectures, in which tasks can be routed across four heterogeneous tiers: Road-Side Units (RSUs) at ground level, Low-Altitude Platforms (LAPs, e.g., UAVs at $\sim$1~km), High-Altitude Platforms (HAPs, stratospheric at $\sim$100~km), and Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites at $\sim$2000~km \cite{hrl_ntn}. Each tier provides a distinct trade-off among coverage radius, compute capacity, propagation delay, and energy cost, collectively offering rich but highly heterogeneous offloading opportunities.

At every time slot, the T-NTN offloading controller must jointly answer three coupled questions: \emph{(i) which tier?} (layer selection from $|\mathcal{L}|=4$ options), \emph{(ii) what fraction?} (continuous offloading ratio $\alpha \in [0,1]$), and \emph{(iii) which specific node?} (combinatorial selection from up to $M = \sum_l M_l$ candidate nodes). This joint decision constitutes a mixed-integer non-convex optimization that grows in hardness with $M$. Classical Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) \cite{hrl_ntn} decomposes the problem into three sequential Deep Q-Networks (DQNs)---one per sub-problem---but this pipeline carries three compounding limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Parameter explosion:} Each DQN input layer has width $n$ (the state dimension), requiring $O(n \cdot h)$ weights per hidden layer of width $h$. Across three DQNs, the total parameter count scales as $O(3\,n \cdot h)$, easily exceeding $10^5$ for realistic $n$ and $h$.
  \item \textbf{Combinatorial intractability:} Node selection within a tier is a combinatorial assignment problem. Treating it as a flat $Q$-value lookup over $M_l$ discrete actions ignores the problem's algebraic structure and degrades as $M_l$ grows, providing no guarantee of near-optimality.
  \item \textbf{Sequential training friction:} Because each DQN's input depends on the upstream network's output, inter-layer coordination requires careful orchestration, leading to instability and slow convergence.
\end{enumerate}

Quantum computing offers structurally different solutions to each limitation. We note that fault-tolerant, error-corrected quantum computers remain a longer-term prospect; the near-term \emph{Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum} (NISQ) era~\cite{preskill2018nisq}, characterized by 50--1000 qubits with non-negligible gate errors and no full error correction, demands algorithms that are both parameter-efficient and noise-resilient. Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) encode an $n$-dimensional state into $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits via Amplitude Encoding, a logarithmic qubit requirement that yields a compact, parameter-efficient policy representation \cite{vqc_drl}. We emphasise at the outset that this work claims a \emph{representational and parameter-efficiency} benefit rather than a quantum computational speedup; the latter would require a formal complexity separation that is beyond the present scope. The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) \cite{farhi2014quantum} is purpose-designed for combinatorial optimization: it encodes the node-selection problem as an Ising Hamiltonian and finds the near-optimal node in $O(p \cdot M_l)$ circuit depth, independent of the DQN parameter count. Bayesian Optimization (BO) \cite{bo_qaoa} replaces noisy gradient descent with a sample-efficient Gaussian Process surrogate, enabling robust circuit parameter tuning on NISQ hardware~\cite{preskill2018nisq}.

Motivated by these complementary strengths, we propose \textbf{Quantum-HRL}, a unified hybrid quantum-classical framework that:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Replaces the three-DQN pipeline with a \emph{single VQC} acting as high-level policy, jointly outputting tier $l^*$ and ratio $\alpha$ from a $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$-qubit register.
  \item Employs \emph{QAOA} to solve the node-selection QUBO within tier $l^*$, exploiting the Hamiltonian structure for near-optimal combinatorial decisions.
  \item Trains the VQC policy with exact Parameter-Shift-Rule policy gradients and applies \emph{Bayesian Optimization} to tune the QAOA angles, achieving robust convergence with few circuit evaluations on noisy hardware.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:notation} consolidates the notation used throughout the paper. Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work and identifies the research gap. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} establishes the quantum computing mathematical foundations required to understand the framework. Section~\ref{sec:model} formalises the T-NTN communication model, per-tier latency and energy formulas, and the QUBO mapping. Section~\ref{sec:framework} details the proposed Quantum-HRL pipeline and reports a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including setup, scenarios, evaluation metrics, results, and an in-depth discussion. Section~\ref{sec:complexity} analyses parameter complexity. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper and outlines future research directions.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Group 1: Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning for Vehicular Edge Computing}

Mobile and vehicular edge computing have become central enablers of latency-sensitive vehicular services, and task offloading is the canonical resource-management problem in this setting~\cite{mao2017mec,liu2021vec}. The integration of non-terrestrial tiers (LAP, HAP, LEO) into terrestrial networks---now a defining feature of 5G-Advanced and 6G architectures~\cite{rinaldi2020ntn,giordani2021ntn,kodheli2021satellite}---adds further heterogeneity in coverage, latency, and energy, motivating learning-based controllers that can adapt across tiers. Task offloading in such multi-tier networks has been addressed with progressively sophisticated reinforcement learning architectures, building on the deep $Q$-network paradigm~\cite{mnih2015dqn} and the broader reinforcement-learning framework~\cite{sutton2018rl}. Early single-tier MEC works relied on Lyapunov optimization or convex relaxation, trading optimality for tractability. As network heterogeneity grew, Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning emerged as a natural decomposition strategy, exploiting temporal and structural abstraction to tame large joint action spaces~\cite{barto2003hrl}. The work in \cite{hrl_ntn} (Shinde and Tarchi) proposed the first three-tier HRL framework for multi-layer, multi-service T-NTN vehicular edge computing, decomposing the joint offloading decision into a master DQN for tier selection and worker DQNs for resource allocation and node assignment. This work established the foundational formulation that we extend and serves as our primary classical baseline. Its critical limitation, however, is that each DQN independently parameterizes the full $n$-dimensional state space---leading to $O(n \cdot h)$ weight growth per network and treating the combinatorial node-selection sub-problem as a flat $Q$-value table, an approach that neither scales gracefully beyond $M_l \approx 10$ nodes nor exploits the algebraic structure of the assignment problem.

Other HRL-based VEC studies have explored actor-critic variants and attention-augmented policies to improve coordination across tiers, yet all inherit the same classical parameter overhead.

\subsection{Group 2: Quantum Computing for Combinatorial Optimization and Reinforcement Learning}

Quantum machine learning has matured rapidly within the NISQ era~\cite{preskill2018nisq}, with variational quantum algorithms emerging as the dominant near-term paradigm~\cite{biamonte2017nature,dunjko2018qml,cerezo2021vqa}. Two streams of this research are directly relevant to our setting.

\textit{Parameterized quantum circuits and quantum reinforcement learning.} Parameterized (variational) quantum circuits act as trainable function approximators whose expressivity derives from data encoding and entanglement~\cite{benedetti2019pqc,schuld2015intro}. The choice of feature map is decisive: amplitude encoding represents an $n$-dimensional state in $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits~\cite{biamonte2017qml}, while feature-space~\cite{havlicek2019feature,schuld2019feature} and data re-uploading~\cite{perezsalinas2020reupload} encodings trade qubit count for circuit depth. Building on these primitives, \cite{vqc_drl} showed that VQCs can serve as function approximators in deep RL, achieving competitive cumulative reward on benchmark tasks (CartPole, LunarLander) with substantially fewer trainable parameters than equivalent classical MLPs; subsequent work extended this to value-based~\cite{skolik2022qdqn} and policy-gradient~\cite{jerbi2021policies} quantum agents. These studies, however, target isolated control benchmarks with no communication or network structure.

\textit{QAOA and quantum combinatorial optimization.} The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm \cite{farhi2014quantum} encodes a combinatorial objective as an Ising Hamiltonian $H_C$~\cite{lucas2014ising} and alternates between cost and mixer unitaries to find near-optimal solutions in $O(p \cdot M)$ circuit depth, where $p$ is the number of QAOA layers and $M$ is the problem size; its performance and mechanism have been characterised extensively~\cite{zhou2020qaoa}, generalised through alternating operator ansätze~\cite{hadfield2019qaoa}, and demonstrated on superconducting hardware~\cite{harrigan2021qaoa}. A key deployment challenge on NISQ hardware is the noisy outer-loop classical optimization of the $2p$ variational angles. Bayesian Optimization---a sample-efficient, derivative-free method built on Gaussian-process surrogates~\cite{rasmussen2006gp,shahriari2016bo,snoek2012bo,frazier2018bo}---is well suited to this regime, and \cite{bo_qaoa} (Tibaldi et al.) demonstrated that using it as the QAOA outer loop substantially reduces the number of circuit evaluations needed for convergence, making practical deployment feasible on today's hardware.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Despite these complementary advances, \textbf{no prior work has integrated VQC-based hierarchical policy learning with QAOA-based combinatorial node selection for task offloading in multi-layer T-NTN environments}. Existing quantum RL studies target isolated benchmark environments with no communication or network constraints; existing T-NTN offloading studies remain fully classical. Quantum-HRL bridges this gap, providing the first end-to-end hybrid quantum-classical framework for the joint tier-node-ratio offloading problem.

\section{Quantum Computing Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

This section establishes, from first principles, the mathematical apparatus underlying the Quantum-HRL framework. Readers already familiar with variational quantum algorithms may proceed directly to Section~\ref{sec:model}. The exposition follows the axiomatic style of~\cite{vqc_drl,farhi2014quantum}, adopting the bra-ket notation defined in the preamble throughout.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Qubit and Hilbert Space Structure}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{postulate}[Qubit state~{\cite{yanofsky2007intro}}]
\label{post:qubit}
The general state of a single qubit is a unit vector in the two-dimensional complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_2 \cong \mathbb{C}^2$:
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1},
  \qquad \alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{C},
  \qquad \abs{\alpha}^2 + \abs{\beta}^2 = 1,
  \label{eq:qubit}
\end{equation}
where $\ket{0} = (1,0)^\top$ and $\ket{1} = (0,1)^\top$ are the \emph{computational basis states}. The coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are probability amplitudes encoding quantum superposition: unless measured, the qubit simultaneously ``occupies'' both basis states.
\end{postulate}

The \emph{Pauli matrices} together with the identity form a complete basis for all $2\times 2$ Hermitian operators~\cite{yanofsky2007intro}:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^x = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad
  \sigma^y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -i\\ i & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad
  \sigma^z = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \quad
  I = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:pauli}
\end{equation}
Their eigenvalues are $\pm 1$, and they satisfy the anti-commutation relation $\{\sigma^\mu, \sigma^\nu\} = 2\delta^{\mu\nu}I$. In particular, $\sigma^z\ket{0} = +\ket{0}$ and $\sigma^z\ket{1} = -\ket{1}$.

For a system of $n$ qubits the composite Hilbert space is the tensor product~\cite{yanofsky2007intro}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}_{2^n} = \mathcal{H}_2^{\otimes n}, \qquad
  \ket{\Psi} = \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} c_x\,\ket{x}, \qquad
  \sum_{x}\abs{c_x}^2 = 1.
  \label{eq:nqubit}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[State-space dimensionality]
\label{prop:parallelism}
An $n$-qubit register spans a $2^n$-dimensional Hilbert space, so a normalised classical vector of dimension up to $2^n$ can be represented using only $n$ qubits. This dimensionality underlies the \emph{compact representation} exploited in this work. We stress that the exponential size of the state space does not by itself constitute a computational speedup: extracting classical information is bounded by measurement (Postulate~\ref{post:born}), and any algorithmic advantage must be established separately.
\end{proposition}

%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Quantum Gates and Measurement}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Quantum gates are unitary operators $U \in \mathcal{U}(2^n)$ that evolve quantum states reversibly. The two gate families relevant to VQC design are:

\begin{definition}[Single-qubit rotation gate]
\label{def:ry}
The rotation about the $Y$-axis by angle $\theta$ is:
\begin{equation}
  R_Y(\theta) = e^{-i\theta\sigma^y/2}
  = \begin{pmatrix}\cos(\theta/2) & -\sin(\theta/2)\\ \sin(\theta/2) & \cos(\theta/2)\end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:ry}
\end{equation}
As $\theta$ varies continuously over $[0, 4\pi)$, $R_Y(\theta)$ traces the full $SU(2)$ group. The parameter $\theta$ is directly trainable via the Parameter-Shift Rule (Section~\ref{subsec:psr}).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[CNOT entangling gate~{\cite{yanofsky2007intro}}]
\label{def:cnot}
The controlled-NOT gate acts on a control qubit $j$ and target qubit $j{+}1$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CNOT}_{j,j+1}
  = \ket{0}\bra{0}_j \otimes I_{j+1}
  + \ket{1}\bra{1}_j \otimes \sigma^x_{j+1}.
  \label{eq:cnot}
\end{equation}
CNOT generates \emph{entanglement}: applied after product-state rotations, it creates correlations between qubits that have no classical analogue and that increase the expressive power of the circuit.
\end{definition}

\begin{postulate}[Measurement and Born rule~{\cite{yanofsky2007intro}}]
\label{post:born}
Measuring qubit $j$ in the computational basis produces outcome $b \in \{0,1\}$ with probability:
\begin{equation}
  P(b_j = b) = \bra{\Psi}\!\left(\ket{b}\bra{b}_j \otimes I_{\bar{j}}\right)\!\ket{\Psi},
  \label{eq:born}
\end{equation}
and the state collapses irreversibly to the corresponding eigenstate. The \emph{expectation value} of an observable $\hat{O}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \langle\hat{O}\rangle = \bra{\Psi}\hat{O}\ket{\Psi} \in \mathbb{R},
  \label{eq:expval}
\end{equation}
estimated in practice by averaging outcomes over $N_{\mathrm{shots}}$ repeated circuit executions.
\end{postulate}

\begin{remark}
All classical outputs of VQC and QAOA in Quantum-HRL are computed as expectation values of Pauli-$Z$ observables. The layer selection $l^*$ and offloading ratio $\alpha$ (Section~\ref{subsec:implementation}) are both derived from $\langle\hat{O}\rangle$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:expval}.
\end{remark}

%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Amplitude Encoding}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Amplitude encoding is the data-loading strategy that enables the logarithmic qubit requirement central to our parameter-efficiency argument.

\begin{definition}[Amplitude encoding~{\cite{biamonte2017qml}}]
\label{def:amp_enc}
Given a normalized classical vector $\tilde{s} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ with $\norm{\tilde{s}}_2 = 1$, the amplitude encoding feature map $\phi$ prepares:
\begin{equation}
  \phi\colon \tilde{s} \mapsto \ket{\tilde{s}}
  = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \tilde{s}_i\,\ket{i} \in \mathcal{H}_{2^{\lceil\log_2 n\rceil}},
  \label{eq:amp_enc_def}
\end{equation}
using only $q = \lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits to represent an $n$-dimensional vector. Preparing this state requires an arbitrary-state-preparation circuit of depth $O(2^q) = O(n)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Logarithmic qubit requirement~{\cite{biamonte2017qml}}]
\label{prop:compression}
Amplitude encoding reduces the qubit count from $O(n)$ (as would be required by basis or angle encoding) to $O(\log_2 n)$. Equivalently, the number of \emph{trainable parameters} in the subsequent VQC scales as $O(\log_2 n)$ rather than $O(n)$, a parameter-efficiency benefit in model size. This is a statement about model compactness, not about runtime complexity.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Induced quantum kernel~{\cite{biamonte2017qml}}]
\label{prop:kernel}
The quantum kernel induced by amplitude encoding is the squared inner product:
\begin{equation}
  \kappa(s, s') = \abs{\braket{s'}{s}}^2 = \abs{s^{\top} s'}^2,
  \label{eq:kernel}
\end{equation}
which equals the square of the linear kernel. This is an \emph{identity feature map}: the encoding itself introduces no non-linearity. Non-linear feature transformation is instead provided by the parameterized VQC layers applied after encoding.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Normalisation requirement]
\label{cor:norm}
Amplitude encoding requires $\norm{s}_2 = 1$. For the environment state $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^n$ used in Section~\ref{subsec:implementation}, the normalised input is $\tilde{s}_t = s_t/\norm{s_t}_2$ before encoding.
\end{corollary}

%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Variational Quantum Circuits and the Parameter-Shift Rule}
\label{subsec:psr}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[VQC ansatz~{\cite{vqc_drl}}]
\label{def:vqc_ansatz}
A Variational Quantum Circuit (VQC) of depth $L$ on $q$ qubits is the parameterized unitary:
\begin{equation}
  U(\theta) = \prod_{\ell=1}^{L}
  \left(\bigotimes_{j=1}^{q} R_Y(\theta_{\ell,j})
  \cdot \prod_{j=1}^{q-1}\mathrm{CNOT}_{j,j+1}\right),
  \label{eq:vqc_def}
\end{equation}
where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{L \cdot q}$ is the vector of trainable angles. Each layer alternates parameterized rotations (which adjust the circuit's action) with fixed CNOT gates (which generate entanglement between qubits). The circuit acts as a \emph{quantum function approximator}: given encoded input $\ket{\psi_0}$, the classical output is $y_k = \langle\hat{O}_k\rangle_\theta = \bra{\psi_0}U(\theta)^\dagger\hat{O}_k\,U(\theta)\ket{\psi_0}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Parameter-Shift Rule~{\cite{mitarai2018psr,vqc_drl}}]
\label{prop:psr}
For any gate of the form $G(\theta_j) = e^{-i\theta_j P/2}$ where $P$ is a Pauli operator with eigenvalues $\pm 1$, the exact gradient of the expectation value $f(\theta) = \langle\hat{O}\rangle_\theta$ with respect to $\theta_j$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta_j}
  = \frac{1}{2}\!\left[f\!\left(\theta_j + \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)
  - f\!\left(\theta_j - \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\right],
  \label{eq:psr}
\end{equation}
requiring exactly two circuit evaluations per parameter. This provides an unbiased, analytic gradient without finite-difference approximation or backpropagation through quantum operations.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Total gradient cost]
For a VQC with $W_{\mathrm{VQC}} = L \cdot q$ parameters, computing the full gradient vector $\nabla_\theta f$ requires exactly $2W_{\mathrm{VQC}} = 2Lq$ circuit evaluations per training step via the Parameter-Shift Rule.
\end{corollary}


%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{QUBO Formulation and Ising Hamiltonian Mapping}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[QUBO problem]
\label{def:qubo_prelim}
A Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem over binary variables $z \in \{0,1\}^m$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{z \in \{0,1\}^m} \; f(z) = z^\top Q\, z + c^\top z,
  \label{eq:qubo_prelim}
\end{equation}
for a real symmetric matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ and offset vector $c \in \mathbb{R}^m$. Constraints are incorporated as quadratic penalty terms with large coefficients added to $Q$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Ising spin mapping]
\label{prop:ising}
The substitution $z_i = (I - \hat{\sigma}_i^z)/2$ maps each binary variable to a Pauli-$Z$ operator, converting the QUBO~\eqref{eq:qubo_prelim} into an \emph{Ising Hamiltonian}:
\begin{equation}
  H_{\mathrm{Ising}} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} h_i\,\hat{\sigma}_i^z
  + \sum_{i < j} J_{ij}\,\hat{\sigma}_i^z\hat{\sigma}_j^z + E_0,
  \label{eq:ising}
\end{equation}
where the local fields $h_i$ and coupling constants $J_{ij}$ are linear functions of $Q$ and $c$, and $E_0$ is a scalar energy offset. The \emph{ground state} (eigenstate of lowest eigenvalue) of $H_{\mathrm{Ising}}$ encodes the optimal QUBO solution $z^*$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
The substitution uses the convention that $\hat{\sigma}^z$ eigenvalues are $\pm 1$: $\hat{\sigma}^z\ket{0} = +\ket{0}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^z\ket{1} = -\ket{1}$. Hence $z_i = 0$ corresponds to spin $+1$ and $z_i = 1$ to spin $-1$.
\end{remark}

%------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA)}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[QAOA state]
\label{def:qaoa}
Given a cost Hamiltonian $H_C$ (the Ising form of the QUBO) and the transverse-field mixer $H_M = \sum_{i=1}^{m}\hat{\sigma}_i^x$, the depth-$p$ QAOA variational state is:
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\gamma,\beta}
  = \prod_{q=1}^{p} e^{-i\beta_q H_M}\,e^{-i\gamma_q H_C}\,\ket{+}^{\otimes m},
  \label{eq:qaoa_def}
\end{equation}
where $\ket{+}^{\otimes m} = H^{\otimes m}\ket{0}^{\otimes m}$ is the uniform superposition initial state and $\{\gamma_q, \beta_q\}_{q=1}^p \in \mathbb{R}^{2p}$ are the $2p$ variational angles to be optimized.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Role of the cost and mixer unitaries]
\label{prop:qaoa_roles}
The cost unitary $e^{-i\gamma_q H_C}$ imprints the problem structure onto the phases of the superposition: computational basis states $\ket{z}$ acquire phase $e^{-i\gamma_q \lambda_z}$ proportional to their cost $\lambda_z = \bra{z}H_C\ket{z}$. The mixer unitary $e^{-i\beta_q H_M}$ redistributes amplitude across basis states, enabling constructive interference near low-cost solutions and destructive interference near high-cost ones.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Convergence with circuit depth]
\label{prop:qaoa_convergence}
As $p \to \infty$ with optimal angles $(\gamma^*, \beta^*)$, the QAOA state converges to the ground state of $H_C$, recovering the exact optimal solution. For finite $p$, measuring $\ket{\gamma^*,\beta^*}$ in the computational basis yields the optimal bitstring $z^*$ with probability that increases monotonically with $p$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Parameter count]
The QAOA circuit introduces only $W_{\mathrm{QAOA}} = 2p$ variational angles, independent of the problem size $m$. This contrasts with a classical $Q$-value table over $m$ discrete actions, which requires $O(m)$ entries.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Bayesian Optimization for NISQ Parameter Tuning}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Current quantum hardware belongs to the \emph{Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum} (NISQ) regime~\cite{preskill2018nisq}: devices with 50--1000 qubits subject to gate errors ($\sim 10^{-3}$ per two-qubit gate), decoherence, and readout noise~\cite{preskill2018nisq}. Fault-tolerant, error-corrected quantum computing remains a longer-term prospect; in the near term, algorithms must be designed to tolerate hardware imperfections. Each circuit evaluation is therefore stochastic, making gradient-based optimization unreliable.

\begin{definition}[Gaussian Process surrogate]
\label{def:gp}
A Gaussian Process (GP) is a probability distribution over functions $f\colon \Theta \to \mathbb{R}$ fully specified by a prior mean $\mu(\theta)$ and covariance kernel $k(\theta, \theta')$:
\begin{equation}
  f(\theta) \sim \mathcal{GP}\!\left(\mu(\theta),\; k(\theta, \theta')\right).
  \label{eq:gp_def}
\end{equation}
Given $t$ noisy observations $\mathcal{D}^{(t)} = \{(\theta^{(i)}, y^{(i)})\}_{i=1}^t$ with $y^{(i)} = f(\theta^{(i)}) + \varepsilon^{(i)}$, the GP posterior $p(f \mid \mathcal{D}^{(t)})$ is also Gaussian and provides a closed-form predictive mean and variance at any query point.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Expected Improvement acquisition]
\label{def:ei}
The Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function selects the next query point by maximizing:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{EI}\!\left(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}^{(t)}\right)
  = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\max\!\left(f(\theta) - f^*,\; 0\right) \mid \mathcal{D}^{(t)}\right],
  \label{eq:ei_def}
\end{equation}
where $f^* = \max_{i \leq t} y^{(i)}$ is the current best observation. EI balances \emph{exploration} (query regions of high posterior uncertainty) against \emph{exploitation} (query regions of high posterior mean).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Sample efficiency on noisy quantum hardware]
\label{prop:bo_efficiency}
In the presence of additive i.i.d.\ Gaussian gate noise $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_n^2)$, Bayesian Optimization with a Mat\'{e}rn kernel achieves $\varepsilon$-accuracy in a $d$-dimensional parameter space using $O(d \log B)$ circuit evaluations within a budget of $B$ queries, compared to $O(B)$ evaluations required by stochastic gradient descent. On NISQ hardware, this translates to an order-of-magnitude reduction in quantum circuit executions~\cite{bo_qaoa}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[BO for the QAOA outer loop]
In Quantum-HRL, Bayesian Optimization tunes the QAOA angles $(\gamma, \beta) \in \mathbb{R}^{2p}$; for $p{=}2$ this is a $d{=}4$-dimensional, well-conditioned GP regression problem whose optimum is typically located within tens of circuit evaluations. The VQC angles $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{Lq}$ are instead trained by exact Parameter-Shift-Rule policy gradients (Section~\ref{subsec:training}), for which the analytic gradient is available; BO is reserved for the QAOA energy landscape, where gradient signals through measurement sampling are least reliable~\cite{bo_qaoa}.
\end{corollary}

\section{System Model and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Network Architecture}

We consider a T-NTN with four tiers indexed by $l \in \mathcal{L} = \{1,2,3,4\}$: RSUs ($l=1$), LAPs ($l=2$), HAPs ($l=3$), and LEO satellites ($l=4$). Tier $l$ contains $M_l$ compute nodes, giving a total of $M = \sum_{l} M_l$ candidate offloading destinations. Additionally, one Cloud Computing facility $\mathcal{C}$ is located at ground level and accessible via backhaul links from each EN; it is capable of providing all services and is used as a fallback when the selected EN cannot serve the requested service type~\cite{hrl_ntn}.

At time slot $\tau_i$, vehicle $k$ generates task $x_k = (d_k, c_k, T_k^{\max}, \xi_k)$, where $d_k$ (bytes) is the payload size, $c_k$ (CPU cycles) is the workload, $T_k^{\max}$ (s) is the hard deadline, and $\xi_k \in \Xi$ is the type of service requested. Each EN $e$ can serve only a subset $\Xi_e \subseteq \Xi$ of services. If the selected EN $e$ cannot serve $\xi_k$ (i.e., $\xi_k \notin \Xi_e$), it relays the task to $\mathcal{C}$, incurring additional communication and computation overhead. The binary indicator $b_e^{\xi_k} = \mathbf{1}[\xi_k \notin \Xi_e]$ flags this relay event.

\subsection{Communication Model}

Following~\cite{hrl_ntn}, the channel gain between vehicle $k$ and any EN $e$ is modeled by distance-dependent path loss with a uniform formula across all tiers:
\begin{equation}
  h_{k,e}(i) = \eta_0 \cdot d_{k,e}(i)^{-\delta}
  \label{eq:channel_gain}
\end{equation}
where $d_{k,e}(i)$ is the Euclidean distance between vehicle $k$ and EN $e$ at time slot $i$, $\eta_0$ is the reference channel power gain at 1~m, and $\delta$ is the path-loss exponent whose value depends on the specific link type (ground-to-vehicle, air-to-ground, or satellite link). The Shannon capacity of the $k$-to-$e$ link is:
\begin{equation}
  R_{k,e}(i) = B_{k,e}(i)\,\log_2\!\left(1 + \frac{P_k\,h_{k,e}(i)}{N_0}\right)
  \label{eq:shannon}
\end{equation}
where $P_k$ is the vehicle transmit power, $B_{k,e}(i)$ is the allocated bandwidth, and $N_0 = N_T B_{k,e}(i)$ is the thermal noise power with spectral density $N_T$. A symmetric channel is assumed between any communicating pair~\cite{hrl_ntn}.

\subsection{Task Offloading Cost Model}

For task $x_k$ with offloading fraction $\alpha_k \in [0,1]$ to EN $e$ in tier $l$, the \textbf{offloading latency} includes transmission, waiting, computation at $e$ (or cloud relay if $b_e^{\xi_k}=1$), and result reception:
\begin{equation}
  T_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i) = \alpha_k\!\left[T_{tx,ke}^{x_k} + T_{w,ke}^{x_k}
    + (1-b_e^{\xi_k})\,T_{c,ke}^{x_k} + T_{rx,ek}^{x_k}
    + b_e^{\xi_k}\!\left(T_{tx,e\mathcal{C}}^{x_k}
    + T_{w,\mathcal{C}}^{x_k} + T_{c,\mathcal{C}}^{x_k}
    + T_{rx,\mathcal{C}k}^{x_k}\right)\right]
  \label{eq:T_off}
\end{equation}
where $T_{tx}$, $T_w$, $T_c$, $T_{rx}$ denote transmission time, waiting time, computation time, and receive time respectively. The \textbf{local computation latency} for the non-offloaded fraction is:
\begin{equation}
  T_{k,\mathrm{loc}}^{x_k}(i) = (1-\alpha_k)\,T_{c,kk}^{x_k}
  \label{eq:T_loc}
\end{equation}
where $T_{c,kk}^{x_k} = c_k / f_{v,k}$ is the time to process the full task locally at CPU frequency $f_{v,k}$. Since partial offloading allows local and edge processing to run \emph{in parallel}, the \textbf{total task latency} is:
\begin{equation}
  T^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\, a_{k,e}\right)
  = \max\!\left(T_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i),\; T_{k,\mathrm{loc}}^{x_k}(i)\right)
  \label{eq:latency}
\end{equation}
Define the VU offloading energy and EN offloading energy as:
\begin{align}
  E_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i) &= \alpha_k\!\left(E_{tx,ke}^{x_k} + E_{rx,ek}^{x_k}\right)
  \label{eq:E_koff}\\
  E_{e,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i) &= \alpha_k\!\left[E_{w,ke}^{x_k}
    + (1-b_e^{\xi_k})\,E_{c,ke}^{x_k}
    + b_e^{\xi_k}\!\left(E_{tx,e\mathcal{C}}^{x_k}
    + E_{rx,\mathcal{C}e}^{x_k}\right)\right]
  \label{eq:E_eoff}
\end{align}
where $E_{tx}$, $E_{rx}$, $E_w$, $E_c$ are base energy costs for transmission, reception, waiting, and computation of the \emph{full} task respectively. The offloading fraction $\alpha_k$ scales both the VU and EN energy contributions. The \textbf{total energy cost} is then~\cite{hrl_ntn}:
\begin{equation}
  E^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\, a_{k,e}\right)
  = w_k\,E_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i)
  + E_{k,\mathrm{loc}}^{x_k}(i)
  + w_e\,E_{e,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i)
  \label{eq:energy}
\end{equation}
where $w_k$ and $w_e$ are VU and EN energy-weighting coefficients, and the local computation energy is $E_{k,\mathrm{loc}}^{x_k}(i) = (1-\alpha_k)\,E_{c,kk}^{x_k}$, with $E_{c,kk}^{x_k}$ denoting the energy required to process the full task $x_k$ locally at the vehicle.

\subsection{Joint Optimization Problem}

The system minimizes the weighted sum of latency and energy over all $K$ vehicles:
\begin{equation}
  \textbf{(P1):} \quad
  \min_{\{a_{k,e},\,\alpha_k\}}
  \;\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}
  \Bigl[\beta_1\,T^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right)
       + \beta_2\,E^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right)\Bigr]
  \label{eq:P1}
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
  \text{s.t.} \quad
    & \sum_{e} a_{k,e} \leq 1, \quad \forall k
      \tag{C1}\label{eq:c1}\\
    & T^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right) \leq T_k^{\max}, \quad \forall k
      \tag{C2}\label{eq:c2}\\
    & T_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i) \leq T_{v_k,\mathrm{soj}}^{e}(i), \quad \forall k,e,i
      \tag{C3}\label{eq:c3}\\
    & E^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right) \leq w_k\,E_{c,kk}^{x_k}, \quad \forall k
      \tag{C4}\label{eq:c4}\\
    & 0 \leq \beta_1,\,\beta_2 \leq 1;\quad \beta_1 + \beta_2 = 1
      \tag{C5}\label{eq:c5}
\end{align}
where $a_{k,e} \in \{0,1\}$ is the binary EN-selection variable and $\beta_1, \beta_2$ are latency/energy weighting coefficients. \textbf{C1} ensures each vehicle selects at most one EN. \textbf{C2} enforces the hard task deadline. \textbf{C3} requires the offloading to complete before the vehicle leaves EN $e$'s coverage (sojourn constraint), where $T_{v_k,\mathrm{soj}}^{e}(i)$ is the vehicle's remaining sojourn time under $e$. \textbf{C4} bounds the total energy cost by the energy needed for full local processing---ensuring offloading is energetically justified. \textbf{C5} normalizes the weighting coefficients. Problem (P1) is NP-hard due to the mixed-integer structure and non-convex coupling between the continuous $\alpha_k$ and discrete $a_{k,e}$.

\subsection{QUBO Formulation for Node Selection}

Given a fixed tier $l^*$ and ratio $\alpha$, node selection reduces to $n^* = \argmin_n [\beta_1 T^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n}) + \beta_2 E^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n})]$, where $T^{x_k}$ and $E^{x_k}$ are evaluated via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:latency} and~\eqref{eq:energy} with the offloading ratio $\alpha$ fixed by the VQC. We reformulate this as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) over $z \in \{0,1\}^{M_{l^*}}$, where $z_n = 1$ iff node $n$ is chosen:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{z} \;\mathcal{H}(z) = \sum_{n=1}^{M_{l^*}} c_n z_n + A\!\left(\sum_{n=1}^{M_{l^*}} z_n - 1\right)^{\!2}
  \label{eq:qubo}
\end{equation}
where $c_n \triangleq \beta_1 T^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n}) + \beta_2 E^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n})$ is the per-node cost evaluated from the parallel-processing latency and weighted-energy model, and penalty $A \gg \max_n c_n$ enforces the one-hot constraint. Substituting $z_n = \tfrac{I - \hat{\sigma}_n^z}{2}$ (Pauli-$Z$ mapping, with $\hat{\sigma}_n^z\ket{0}=+\ket{0}$, $\hat{\sigma}_n^z\ket{1}=-\ket{1}$) and expanding, one obtains the Ising-form cost Hamiltonian consumed by QAOA:
\begin{equation}
  H_C = \sum_{n=1}^{M_{l^*}} h_n\,\hat{\sigma}_n^z
        + \frac{A}{2}\!\sum_{n < m}\!\hat{\sigma}_n^z\hat{\sigma}_m^z + E_0
  \label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
with local fields, couplings, and constant offset:
\begin{equation}
  h_n = -\frac{c_n}{2} + A\!\left(1 - \frac{M_{l^*}}{2}\right),
  \quad
  J_{nm} = \frac{A}{2},
  \quad
  E_0 = \sum_n \frac{c_n}{2} + A\!\left(1 - \frac{M_{l^*}}{2} + \frac{M_{l^*}(M_{l^*}-1)}{4}\right).
  \label{eq:ising_coeffs}
\end{equation}
The positive coupling $J_{nm} = +A/2 > 0$ penalises states where two or more spins simultaneously reach $-1$ (i.e., two nodes co-selected), enforcing the one-hot constraint. The ground state of $H_C$ identifies the minimum-cost node; Section~\ref{subsec:implementation} details how QAOA finds this ground state.


\subsection{MDP Formulation}

We cast (P1) as an MDP $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{R}, \gamma_{\mathrm{MDP}})$ following the hierarchical decomposition of~\cite{hrl_ntn}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{State} $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^n$: A concatenation of (i) vehicle position and velocity, (ii) per-node CPU load $\{u_{l,n}^t\}$, (iii) channel gains $\{h_{k,e}^t\}$, (iv) layer-wise average VU count and EN mobility estimate, and (v) task parameters $(d_k, c_k, T_k^{\max}, \xi_k)$. The state dimension is $n = 20$ for the experimental configuration of Section~\ref{subsec:setup}.
  \item \textbf{Action} $a_t = (l_t, n_t, \alpha_t)$: Jointly selected tier $l_t \in \mathcal{L}$, edge node $n_t \in \{1,\ldots,M_{l_t}\}$, and offloading ratio $\alpha_t \in [0,1]$.
  \item \textbf{Reward} $R_t$: Designed to simultaneously minimize latency and energy while penalizing violations of three operational constraints inherited from~\cite{hrl_ntn}:
\end{itemize}

Define three binary constraint-violation indicators following~\cite{hrl_ntn}:
\begin{align}
  F^1_{k,e}(t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[T_{k,\mathrm{off}}^{x_k}(i) > T_{v_k,\mathrm{soj}}^{e}(i)\right]
  \label{eq:f1} \\
  F^2_{k,e}(t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[T^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right) > T_k^{\max}\right]
  \label{eq:f2} \\
  F^3_{k,e}(t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[E^{x_k}\!\left(\alpha_k,\,a_{k,e}\right) > w_k\,E_{c,kk}^{x_k}\right]
  \label{eq:f3}
\end{align}
where $T_{v_k,\mathrm{soj}}^{e}(i)$ is the sojourn-time bound (offloading must complete before the vehicle leaves EN $e$'s coverage), $T_k^{\max}$ is the hard task deadline, and $w_k E_{c,kk}^{x_k}$ is the energy cost of processing the task entirely locally---so $F^3=1$ signals that offloading is \emph{less} energy-efficient than local computation, violating constraint~\eqref{eq:c4}. Each indicator equals $1$ when the corresponding constraint is violated. The reward is the negated cost from~\eqref{eq:P1} augmented with constraint penalties:
\begin{equation}
  R_t = -\!\left(\beta_1\,T^{x_k} + \beta_2\,E^{x_k}\right)
        - w_1 F^1_{k,e}(t)
        - w_2 F^2_{k,e}(t)
        - w_3 F^3_{k,e}(t),
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_1, \beta_2$ are the weighting coefficients from~\eqref{eq:P1}, $w_1, w_2, w_3 > 0$ are penalty magnitudes for sojourn, deadline, and energy constraint violations respectively, and the negation converts the minimization objective into a maximization reward. Note that~\cite{hrl_ntn} expresses the same objective as a positive cost to minimize; here we negate it to conform to the standard RL convention of maximizing $\mathbb{E}[\sum_t \gamma^t R_t]$. Setting $w_i \gg \max(\beta_1 T^{x_k} + \beta_2 E^{x_k})$ ensures feasibility is prioritized over cost minimization.

\subsection{Proposed Framework: System Implementation}
\label{subsec:implementation}

The proposed Quantum-HRL pipeline replaces the classical multi-DQN architecture of~\cite{hrl_ntn} with a hybrid quantum-classical system composed of four integrated modules---quantum state encoding, a VQC-based high-level policy, a QAOA-based low-level combinatorial optimiser, and a Bayesian-Optimisation outer loop---as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}. Throughout, classical and quantum sub-routines are coupled through a single MDP loop so that policy updates remain consistent with the offloading cost and reward signals defined in Section~\ref{sec:model}.

The classical environment encodes the state $s_t$ into a quantum register via Amplitude Encoding; the VQC produces the high-level decision $(l^*,\alpha)$; the QAOA solver returns the optimal node $n^*$; the Bayesian-Optimisation loop refines all variational angles using the two reward signals $R_1$ and $R_2$ collected in the replay buffer.}
  \label{fig:architecture}

\subsubsection{Quantum State Encoding}

At each time slot $\tau_i$, the environment snapshot $s_t \in \R^n$ is mapped into a quantum state $\ket{\psi(s_t)}$ using \emph{Amplitude Encoding}~\cite{biamonte2017qml}. Given a normalized state vector $\tilde{s}_t = s_t / \norm{s_t}$, we prepare:
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\psi(s_t)} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \tilde{s}_t^{(i)} \ket{i}
  \label{eq:amplitude_encoding}
\end{equation}
This encoding requires only $\lceil \log_2 n \rceil$ qubits to represent an $n$-dimensional state, a logarithmic qubit requirement that yields a compact input representation compared to classical input layers.

\subsubsection{High-Level Strategy with Variational Quantum Circuits}

The encoded quantum state $\ket{\psi(s_t)}$ is processed by the VQC $U(\theta)$, which functions as the high-level policy $\pi_\theta$. The circuit consists of $L$ alternating layers of parameterized single-qubit rotations and entangling CNOT gates:
\begin{equation}
  U(\theta) = \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} \left( \bigotimes_{j} R_Y(\theta_{\ell,j}) \cdot \text{CNOT}_{j,j+1} \right)
  \label{eq:vqc}
\end{equation}

Measuring the output state $U(\theta)\ket{\psi(s_t)}$ yields two outputs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Layer Selection}: The index $l^* = \argmax_{l \in \mathcal{L}} \langle \hat{O}_l \rangle$ derived from expectation values of layer-specific observables $\hat{O}_l$.
  \item \textbf{Offloading Ratio}: $\alpha = \sigma(\langle \hat{O}_\alpha \rangle)$, where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function applied to the expectation value of a dedicated observable.
\end{itemize}


The VQC parameters $\theta$ are updated via the \emph{Parameter-Shift Rule}~\cite{mitarai2018psr}, which provides an unbiased gradient estimator:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \langle \hat{O} \rangle}{\partial \theta_j} = \frac{1}{2}\left[ \langle \hat{O} \rangle_{\theta_j + \frac{\pi}{2}} - \langle \hat{O} \rangle_{\theta_j - \frac{\pi}{2}} \right]
  \label{eq:parameter_shift}
\end{equation}
enabling exact gradient computation without finite-difference approximations.

\subsubsection{Low-Level Optimisation via QAOA}

Once the VQC outputs the joint decision $(l^*, \alpha)$, QAOA is invoked to solve the node-selection QUBO formulated in \eqref{eq:qubo}.

\begin{remark}[Hierarchical ordering rationale]
The classical HRL baseline~\cite{hrl_ntn} sequences decisions as tier (P1) $\to$ node (P2) $\to$ ratio (P3), so the ratio is optimized after the specific node is known. Quantum-HRL instead collapses tier and ratio into a single VQC step, then resolves node selection via QAOA. This ordering is self-consistent: the QAOA cost Hamiltonian is built using $c_n(\alpha) = \beta_1 T_{k,n}(\alpha) + \beta_2 E_{k,n}(\alpha)$, so node selection is performed \emph{conditioned on} the ratio $\alpha$ already output by the VQC. The VQC therefore learns a policy that is globally consistent with the downstream node costs, while QAOA optimizes the combinatorial assignment given the fixed context $(l^*, \alpha)$.
\end{remark}

The one-hot penalty structure ensures that exactly one node is selected, and the Pauli-$Z$ substitution (Section~\ref{sec:model}) yields the cost Hamiltonian $H_C$ in \eqref{eq:hamiltonian}, which encodes all per-node latency and energy costs as eigenvalues. The QAOA circuit of depth $p$ applies alternating unitaries:
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\gamma, \beta} = \prod_{q=1}^{p} e^{-i\beta_q H_M} e^{-i\gamma_q H_C} \ket{+}^{\otimes M_{l^*}}
  \label{eq:qaoa}
\end{equation}
where $H_M = \sum_n \hat{\sigma}_n^x$ is the mixer Hamiltonian and $\ket{+}^{\otimes M_{l^*}}$ is the uniform superposition. Measuring $\ket{\gamma^*, \beta^*}$ in the computational basis yields the optimal bitstring $z^*$, identifying the selected node $n^* = \argmax_n z_n^*$.


\subsubsection{Bayesian Optimisation for Parameter Tuning}

Both the VQC parameters $\theta$ and the QAOA angles $(\gamma, \beta)$ require classical optimization. On NISQ devices, each circuit evaluation is noisy and expensive. We employ Bayesian Optimization (BO) as the classical subroutine \cite{bo_qaoa}, which models the reward landscape $f(\theta)$ with a Gaussian Process (GP) prior:
\begin{equation}
  f(\theta) \sim \mathcal{GP}\left(\mu(\theta), \, k(\theta, \theta')\right)
  \label{eq:gp}
\end{equation}
At each BO iteration, the next query point $\theta^{(t+1)}$ is selected by maximizing the Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function:
\begin{equation}
  \theta^{(t+1)} = \argmax_\theta \; \mathrm{EI}\!\left(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}^{(t)}\right)
  \label{eq:ei}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{D}^{(t)}$ is the set of previous evaluations. BO reduces the number of required circuit evaluations by up to an order of magnitude compared to gradient descent in the presence of gate-level noise, ensuring robust convergence on current-generation quantum hardware.

\subsubsection{Execution and Learning Loop}

The complete Quantum-HRL loop is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:quantum_hrl}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Quantum-HRL for T-NTN Task Offloading}
\label{alg:quantum_hrl}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require T-NTN environment; VQC depth $L$; QAOA depth $p$; BO budget $B$
\Ensure Optimized parameters $\theta^*$, $(\gamma^*, \beta^*)$
\State Initialize $\theta$, $\gamma$, $\beta$ randomly; replay buffer $\mathcal{D} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\For{each episode}
  \State Observe state $s_t$; normalize to $\tilde{s}_t$
  \State Encode: $\ket{\psi(s_t)} \leftarrow$ Amplitude Encoding($\tilde{s}_t$)
  \State \textbf{High-level}: Measure $U(\theta)\ket{\psi(s_t)}$ $\to$ $(l^*, \alpha)$ \Comment{VQC}
  \State Construct cost Hamiltonian $H_C$ for layer $l^*$
  \State \textbf{Low-level}: Run QAOA$(\gamma, \beta)$ on $H_C$ $\to$ $n^*$ \Comment{QAOA}
  \State Execute action $a_t = (l^*, n^*, \alpha)$; observe $R_t$, $s_{t+1}$
  \State Store $(s_t, a_t, R_t, s_{t+1})$ in $\mathcal{D}$
  \If{$|\mathcal{D}|$ mod $B$ = 0}
    \State Update $\theta$ via advantage-weighted PSR policy gradient on a batch from $\mathcal{D}$ \Comment{Eq.~\eqref{eq:vqc_pg}}
    \State Update $(\gamma, \beta)$ via BO on QAOA cost landscape
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The system executes the final action $a_t = (l^*, n^*, \alpha)$ within the T-NTN environment. The resulting latency $T_k$, energy $E_k$, and constraint flags $F^1,F^2,F^3$ are measured to compute the full reward $R_t$ as defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:reward}.

\subsubsection{Training Procedure and Reward--Parameter Coupling}
\label{subsec:training}

\paragraph{Step 1 --- Action selection with \texorpdfstring{$\varepsilon$}{epsilon}-greedy exploration.}

At each environment step $t$, the VQC defines a \emph{stochastic} high-level policy: the tier is sampled from the categorical distribution $\pi_\theta(l \mid s_t) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\langle\hat O_1\rangle,\ldots,\langle\hat O_{|\mathcal L|}\rangle\right)_l$, and the ratio from a Gaussian policy $\alpha \sim \mathcal N\!\left(\mu_\theta(s_t), \sigma_\alpha^2\right)$ clipped to $(0,1]$, where $\mu_\theta(s_t) = \sigma(\langle\hat O_\alpha\rangle)$ is the sigmoid readout. During training, exploration is provided directly by this on-policy sampling; at evaluation time the policy acts greedily ($l^* = \argmax_l \pi_\theta$, $\alpha = \mu_\theta$). QAOA is then called unconditionally to select node $n^*$ within tier $l^*$.

\paragraph{Step 2 --- Reward computation.}

After executing $(l^*, n^*, \alpha)$, the environment returns $T^{x_k}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:latency}) and $E^{x_k}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:energy}). Three binary constraint flags are evaluated:

\begin{equation}
  F^i_{k,n^*}(t) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{constraint } i \text{ violated} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
  \quad i \in \{1,2,3\}
  \label{eq:flags}
\end{equation}

where violation conditions are given in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:f1}--\eqref{eq:f3} (note $F^3_{k,e}=1$ when $E^{x_k} > w_k E_{c,kk}^{x_k}$, i.e., total offloading energy exceeds the local-processing energy). Two reward signals are computed immediately:

\begin{align}
  R_1^t &= -\!\left(\beta_1\,T^{x_k} + \beta_2\,E^{x_k}\right)
           - w_1 F^1 - w_2 F^2 - w_3 F^3
           \label{eq:r1} \\[4pt]
  R_2^t &= -\!\left(\beta_1\,T^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n^*})
           + \beta_2\,E^{x_k}(\alpha, a_{k,n^*})\right)
           - w_2 F^2 - w_3 F^3
           \label{eq:r2}
\end{align}

$R_1^t$ is the \textbf{global reward}: it penalizes the full joint cost of tier, node, and ratio decisions together with all three constraint violations, and is used to train the VQC. $R_2^t$ is the \textbf{intrinsic node reward}: it isolates the contribution of node selection (holding tier and ratio fixed), omits the sojourn penalty $w_1 F^1$ (which is a mobility side effect, not directly attributable to node quality), and is used to score QAOA performance.

\noindent\textbf{Penalty magnitude rule.} To ensure that constraint feasibility is always prioritized over cost minimization, penalty weights must satisfy:
\begin{equation}
  w_i \;\gg\; \max_{k,t}\!\left(\beta_1 T_k + \beta_2 E_k\right),
  \quad i \in \{1,2,3\}.
  \label{eq:penalty_rule}
\end{equation}
In practice, each $w_i$ is set to the empirical 99th-percentile of $(\beta_1 T_k + \beta_2 E_k)$ measured during a random-policy warmup phase. This ensures that even a single constraint violation dominates the reward signal, forcing the policy to treat feasibility as a hard requirement.

\paragraph{Step 3 --- Transition storage.}

The tuple $(s_t,\; l^*,\; n^*,\; \alpha,\; R_1^t,\; R_2^t,\; s_{t+1})$ is appended to the replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$ (capacity $|\mathcal{D}|_{\max}$). The replay buffer breaks temporal correlations between consecutive samples, enabling stable off-policy learning---the same technique used in the three DQN replay memories of the baseline~\cite{hrl_ntn}.

\paragraph{Step 4 --- VQC parameter update (every $B$ steps).}

A mini-batch of $k$ transitions is sampled from $\mathcal{D}$ and the VQC policy is updated by \textbf{advantage-weighted policy gradient} (REINFORCE with a batch-mean baseline). For each sampled transition the normalised advantage is
\begin{equation}
  \hat A_t \;=\; \frac{R_1^t - \bar R_1}{\mathrm{std}(R_1) + \epsilon},
  \qquad \bar R_1 = \tfrac1k\textstyle\sum_{\mathcal D} R_1^t,
  \label{eq:advantage}
\end{equation}
and the ascent direction is the advantage-weighted gradient of the log-policy,
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta J(\theta)
  \;=\; \frac1k \sum_{\mathcal D} \hat A_t
  \Bigl[\, \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(l^*_t \mid s_t)
        \;+\; \nabla_\theta \log \mathcal N\!\bigl(\alpha_t;\, \mu_\theta(s_t), \sigma_\alpha^2\bigr) \Bigr].
  \label{eq:vqc_pg}
\end{equation}
Both log-policy gradients reduce, via the chain rule through the softmax and sigmoid readouts, to linear combinations of the observable gradients $\partial\langle\hat O_k\rangle/\partial\theta_j$, each of which is computed \emph{exactly} with the Parameter-Shift Rule (Eq.~\eqref{eq:psr})---no backpropagation through quantum operations is required. Normalising the advantage in Eq.~\eqref{eq:advantage} is essential on quantum hardware: the raw return $R_1$ (which includes penalty terms of magnitude $w_i$) is unbounded, whereas all circuit outputs are Pauli-$Z$ expectations confined to $[-1,1]$; the normalised advantage makes the update scale-free and keeps the bounded policy readouts well-conditioned.

\paragraph{Step 5 --- QAOA angle update (every $B$ steps).}

The QAOA angles $(\gamma, \beta)$ are \emph{not} updated via the policy gradient. Instead, they are optimized by Bayesian Optimisation to minimize the expected QAOA energy for the cost Hamiltonian constructed from $R_2$ feedback:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute the \textbf{empirical node cost} from recent $R_2$ samples:
    $\hat{c}_n = -\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}[R_2^t \mid n^* = n]$
    (average intrinsic reward received when node $n$ was selected).
  \item Rebuild the \textbf{cost Hamiltonian} $H_C$ using $\hat{c}_n$ as node costs in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qubo}.
  \item Run \textbf{Bayesian Optimization} to minimize:
    \begin{equation}
      C(\gamma, \beta) = \bra{\gamma,\beta} H_C \ket{\gamma,\beta}
      \label{eq:qaoa_cost}
    \end{equation}
    updating the GP surrogate and proposing the next $(\gamma, \beta)$ via EI.
\end{enumerate}

This feedback loop means QAOA learns \emph{which nodes are genuinely good} from accumulated environment experience (via $R_2$), rather than relying solely on the instantaneous cost Hamiltonian. Minimizing $C(\gamma,\beta)$ drives the measured bitstring distribution toward the minimum-cost node, maximizing the probability of selecting $n^* = \argmin_n \hat{c}_n$.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:setup}

\paragraph{Network topology and mobility.}
Following the topology of Shinde and Tarchi~\cite{hrl_ntn}, we instantiate a four-tier T-NTN over a $5$-km urban road network with a single fallback Cloud facility~$\mathcal{C}$. Tier altitudes are taken directly from~\cite[Table~3]{hrl_ntn}: LAPs at $h\!\approx\!1\,$km, HAPs at $h\!\approx\!100\,$km, and LEO satellites at $h\!\approx\!2000\,$km, with RSU coverage $50\,$m, LAP coverage $200\,$m, and HAP coverage $1\,000\,$m. To enable a deterministic action space for the high-level VQC head we additionally fix the per-tier deployment to $M_1{=}5$ RSUs, $M_2{=}3$ LAPs, $M_3{=}2$ HAPs, and $M_4{=}2$ LEO satellites (the baseline of~\cite{hrl_ntn} instantiates node counts dynamically from the coverage radius and road length; our fixed choice is a moderate extension and is held constant across all compared methods). Vehicular users follow the speed model of~\cite[Table~3]{hrl_ntn}, drawn from $\mathcal{U}(8,14)\,$m/s, conditioned on the road segment.

\paragraph{Task and traffic model.}
At each time slot $\tau_i$, every vehicle $k$ generates an independent task $x_k=(d_k,c_k,T_k^{\max},\xi_k)$. Whereas~\cite{hrl_ntn} uses a single nominal task profile ($d_k{=}5\,$Mbytes, $T_k^{\max}{=}4\,$s, $c_k{=}10^3 d_k\,$FLOPs), we extend their setup to a heterogeneous workload by sampling $d_k\!\sim\!\mathcal{U}(0.5,5)\,$Mbytes, $c_k\!\sim\!\mathcal{U}(100,1000)\,$Mcycles, and $T_k^{\max}\!\sim\!\mathcal{U}(0.5,2)\,$s in order to stress the VQC's ability to react to heterogeneous demands. The service type is sampled uniformly from $|\Xi|{=}6$ classes as in~\cite{hrl_ntn}; each EN supports a fixed random subset $\Xi_e\subseteq\Xi$ that mandates cloud relay whenever $\xi_k\notin\Xi_e$, as captured by the indicator $b_e^{\xi_k}$.

\paragraph{Communication parameters.}
The path-loss reference $\eta_0$, the exponent $\delta$, and the per-tier bandwidth $B_{k,e}$ follow the values reported in~\cite{hrl_ntn}; transmit power, noise spectral density, and CPU frequencies of the ENs are likewise taken from that reference. Inter-tier disparities (e.g., LEO latency floors, HAP coverage radius) are therefore preserved.

\paragraph{Quantum-HRL hyperparameters.}
The state vector has dimension $n{=}20$ (encoding position, velocity, $\{u_{l,n}^t\}$, $\{h_{k,e}^t\}$, task descriptor, and per-tier mobility statistics; see Section~\ref{sec:model}), requiring $q{=}\lceil\log_2 20\rceil{=}5$ qubits. The VQC depth is fixed to $L{=}4$ and the QAOA depth to $p{=}2$ unless otherwise stated; the QAOA Bayesian-Optimisation budget is $B{=}20$ acquisition queries per update with a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ GP kernel and Expected-Improvement acquisition, and the VQC policy gradient uses learning rates $(0.12, 0.6)$ for the tier and ratio readouts with Gaussian ratio-policy width $\sigma_\alpha{=}0.3$. Penalty weights $w_1,w_2,w_3$ are calibrated on a random-policy warm-up phase to the empirical $99$th percentile of $(\beta_1 T_k+\beta_2 E_k)$, as specified by Eq.~\eqref{eq:penalty_rule}. The MDP discount is $\gamma_{\mathrm{MDP}}{=}0.95$, and training-time exploration is provided by on-policy sampling from the stochastic policy itself. All experiments are repeated over five independent seeds, and confidence bands report $\pm 1$ standard deviation across seeds.

\paragraph{Software stack and quantum back-end.}
The full simulator is implemented in Python on top of NumPy for the classical environment and a PennyLane statevector simulator for the VQC and QAOA primitives; the inner training loops use analytic (exact) expectation values for tractable turnaround, while finite-shot effects ($N_{\mathrm{shots}}{=}1024$ per observable) and hardware-style Gaussian readout noise are studied separately in the NISQ-noise scenario (Section~\ref{subsec:scenarios}). The Bayesian Optimiser is built on a Mat\'ern-$5/2$ Gaussian-process surrogate (scikit-learn) with Expected-Improvement acquisition \cite{bo_qaoa}. The codebase, configuration files, and random seeds will be released upon publication to ensure full reproducibility.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare Quantum-HRL against:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Classical HRL}~\cite{hrl_ntn}: three sequential DQNs that successively select tier, node, and ratio.
  \item \textbf{Single DQN}: a flat agent with joint action space $|\mathcal{L}|\!\times\!\max_l M_l\!\times\!K_\alpha$, where $K_\alpha{=}11$ ratio bins.
  \item \textbf{Greedy}: deterministic policy that always offloads to the nearest RSU with $\alpha{=}1$, mimicking a connectivity-only heuristic.
  \item \textbf{Random}: uniform random action selection, acting as a lower bound on rational behaviour.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiment Scenarios}
\label{subsec:scenarios}

To validate Quantum-HRL under realistic operating conditions and to ensure that results are reproducible by an independent reader, we formalise the experimental procedure as a sequence of clearly-defined stages. The same data-input, processing, decision-making and output pipeline is applied to every method under comparison; only the high-level/low-level policy is swapped, isolating the contribution of the proposed framework.

\paragraph{(S0) Input data flow.}
At slot $\tau_i$ the simulator generates the following input streams (illustrated by the green arrows in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}):
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Mobility update}: each VU $k$ advances its position $(\mathbf{r}_k^{t},\mathbf{v}_k^{t})$ according to the truncated-Gaussian speed model; sojourn times $T_{v_k,\mathrm{soj}}^{e}(i)$ are recomputed for every $(k,e)$ pair.
  \item \emph{Task generation}: vehicles independently sample $x_k=(d_k,c_k,T_k^{\max},\xi_k)$ from the distributions of Section~\ref{subsec:setup}.
  \item \emph{Channel sounding}: path-loss-based gains $\{h_{k,e}^t\}$ are recomputed using Eq.~\eqref{eq:channel_gain} for the new VU--EN geometry.
  \item \emph{Load probing}: each EN reports its current CPU utilisation $\{u_{l,n}^t\}$ and pending-task queue length.
  \item \emph{State assembly}: the values above are concatenated into the raw state vector $s_t\in\mathbb{R}^{20}$, which is finally normalised to $\tilde s_t = s_t/\norm{s_t}_2$ so that it meets the amplitude-encoding precondition (Corollary~\ref{cor:norm}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{(S1) Processing pipeline.}
The normalised state $\tilde s_t$ is propagated through the Quantum-HRL stack as follows:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{State loading.} Amplitude encoding prepares $\ket{\psi(s_t)}=\sum_i \tilde s_t^{(i)}\ket{i}$ on a $5$-qubit register, compressing the $n{=}20$ dimensional input into $\lceil\log_2 20\rceil$ qubits (Eq.~\eqref{eq:amplitude_encoding}).
  \item \emph{High-level decision.} The VQC $U(\theta)$ acts on $\ket{\psi(s_t)}$; measuring layer-specific observables yields the tier choice $l^*=\arg\max_l\langle\hat O_l\rangle$ and the offloading fraction $\alpha=\sigma(\langle\hat O_\alpha\rangle)$.
  \item \emph{QUBO construction.} Conditioned on $(l^*,\alpha)$, the simulator evaluates the per-node costs $c_n(\alpha)=\beta_1 T^{x_k}(\alpha,a_{k,n})+\beta_2 E^{x_k}(\alpha,a_{k,n})$ from the per-tier latency/energy model and instantiates the Ising Hamiltonian of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian}.
  \item \emph{Low-level decision.} The QAOA circuit~\eqref{eq:qaoa} with current angles $(\gamma,\beta)$ is executed, and the bitstring obtained from the highest-probability measurement identifies the selected node $n^*$.
  \item \emph{Action commit.} The composite action $a_t=(l^*,n^*,\alpha)$ is dispatched to the environment.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{(S2) Decision-making mechanism.}
Training-time exploration is provided by on-policy sampling from the stochastic VQC policy on $(l^*,\alpha)$ (Section~\ref{subsec:training}, Step~1), while the QAOA solver is always invoked. The two reward signals $R_1^t,R_2^t$ (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:r1}--\eqref{eq:r2}) are stored together with the transition tuple $(s_t,l^*,n^*,\alpha,R_1^t,R_2^t,s_{t+1})$ in the replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$. Every $B$ environment steps the outer loop performs (i)~an advantage-weighted PSR policy-gradient update of $\theta$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:vqc_pg}), and (ii)~a Bayesian-Optimisation update of $C(\gamma,\beta)$ for the QAOA angles. Penalty calibration enforces the precedence order \emph{feasibility} $\gg$ \emph{cost} so that any constraint violation systematically dominates the reward (see Table~\ref{tab:penalty_effect}).

\paragraph{(S3) System output.}
At inference time the system outputs, for each task, the triple $(l^*,n^*,\alpha)$ together with the measured latency $T^{x_k}$, total energy $E^{x_k}$, the three violation flags $(F^1,F^2,F^3)$, and the realised rewards $(R_1^t,R_2^t)$. These quantities are logged per slot and aggregated into per-episode and per-epoch summaries that feed the evaluation pipeline (Section~\ref{subsec:metrics}).

\paragraph{(S4) Scenarios under evaluation.}
We evaluate Quantum-HRL on four complementary scenarios designed to stress different aspects of the framework:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Baseline scenario (B)} --- the default T-NTN topology and traffic model of Section~\ref{subsec:setup}, used for the main performance comparison.
  \item \emph{Scalability scenario (Sc)} --- node populations scaled by a factor $\zeta\in\{1,2,4\}$ to probe how the framework behaves as the action space grows. Because the QAOA register grows with $M_{l^*}$, this scenario quantifies the practical reach of the algorithm before NISQ qubit budgets become binding.
  \item \emph{Heavy-load scenario (HL)} --- task arrival rate increased to drive ENs close to saturation, exposing the queuing component $T_w$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:T_off} and the energy-versus-latency trade-off encoded by $(\beta_1,\beta_2)$.
  \item \emph{NISQ-noise scenario (N)} --- additive Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2)$ is injected into every observable estimate to mimic gate and readout errors; $\sigma_n$ is swept over the range observed on current superconducting devices~\cite{preskill2018nisq}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{(S5) Reproducibility conditions.}
All five seeds, hyperparameters, and the deterministic mobility/task generators are documented in the configuration file released with the repository. A complete five-seed training+evaluation run with the default budget (including the ablation sweep) completes in under one hour on a single commodity workstation (no GPU), so that the experimental pipeline is feasible to re-execute end-to-end.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

The empirical study uses both task-level and learning-level metrics, capturing offloading quality, training behaviour, and model efficiency.

\paragraph{Task-level performance.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Average task latency} $\bar T=\mathbb{E}[T^{x_k}]$ (s) computed over all completed tasks in the evaluation window; lower is better.
  \item \emph{Average task energy} $\bar E=\mathbb{E}[E^{x_k}]$ (J) under the weighted model of Eq.~\eqref{eq:energy}.
  \item \emph{Deadline-miss rate} $\Pr(F^2{=}1)$ and \emph{sojourn-violation rate} $\Pr(F^1{=}1)$, both reported in $\%$; they assess feasibility under the constraints (C2)--(C3).
  \item \emph{Energy-suboptimality rate} $\Pr(F^3{=}1)$ that measures how often offloading consumes more energy than local execution (C4).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Learning behaviour.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Cumulative reward curve} $\sum_{t}\gamma_{\mathrm{MDP}}^{t}R_1^t$ versus training episode, used for convergence comparison.
  \item \emph{Episodes-to-plateau} $\Pi_\eta$: number of episodes until the moving-average reward reaches $\eta\%$ of its asymptotic value (typically $\eta{=}95$).
  \item \emph{Seed-wise variance} of the converged reward, indicative of training stability under NISQ-style stochasticity.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Model efficiency.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
  \item \emph{Trainable-parameter count} $W$ (cf.\ Section~\ref{sec:complexity}) and \emph{parameter reduction factor} $\rho=W_{\mathrm{HRL}}/W_{\mathrm{Q\text{-}HRL}}$.
  \item \emph{Number of circuit evaluations per update}, which is the dominant cost on NISQ hardware.
  \item \emph{Wall-clock training time per episode} on the simulated back-end.
\end{itemize}

Under the scalability scenario, doubling and quadrupling the per-tier node populations leaves the VQC parameter count unchanged (because amplitude encoding is logarithmic in $n$) and adds only $O(M_{l^*})$ qubits to the QAOA register. \subsection{Discussion}
\label{subsec:discussion}

We now interpret the empirical observations and the structural properties of Quantum-HRL beyond the headline numbers.

\paragraph{Why does the framework work?}
Three structural facts conspire to deliver the observed gains. \emph{(i)~Information-dense state encoding.} Amplitude encoding maps the full $n$-dimensional state into $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits, so that a single VQC observable already aggregates information from all classical features (Proposition~\ref{prop:compression}); the policy can react to subtle cross-feature interactions (e.g., load--mobility correlation) that the per-DQN baseline must learn implicitly through wide hidden layers. \emph{(ii)~Structure-aware combinatorial search.} QAOA exploits the QUBO algebra directly: the cost Hamiltonian~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian} encodes both per-node cost and the one-hot constraint, and a constant-depth circuit drives the wavefunction toward the ground state regardless of $M_{l^*}$. Treating the same problem as a flat $Q$-value table~\cite{hrl_ntn} ignores this algebraic structure and thus pays a $|\mathcal A_2|$-fold parameter cost. \emph{(iii)~Noise-aware variational tuning.} Bayesian Optimisation is the only outer-loop element that explicitly models the stochasticity of NISQ measurements; this turns hardware noise into a quantifiable observation variance rather than a destabilising perturbation of stochastic gradients.

\paragraph{Strengths relative to baselines.}
Compared with the tri-DQN HRL of~\cite{hrl_ntn}, Quantum-HRL (a)~collapses the high-level pipeline into a single quantum step, removing the inter-network coordination delay; (b)~separates the combinatorial sub-problem from the differentiable policy, so that node selection no longer competes with tier/ratio learning for the same parameter budget; and (c)~replaces per-network target copies and gradient accumulators by a memory-light Gaussian process. Compared with the single-DQN baseline, our hierarchical decomposition enjoys the standard credit-assignment advantages of HRL while avoiding its parameter blow-up.

\paragraph{The latency--energy trade-off.}
The headline result---$20.3\%$ lower latency at roughly $2.8\times$ the per-task energy of the classical baseline---deserves an explicit mechanistic account. The VQC ratio policy converges to aggressive offloading ($\alpha \approx 0.8$--$0.97$), which minimises the parallel-processing latency $\max(T_{\mathrm{off}}, T_{\mathrm{loc}})$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:latency} by shrinking the local branch to near zero, but pays the transmission energy $E_{tx}+E_{rx}$ and the edge-compute energy $E_{c,ke}$ for almost the entire workload. The classical HRL baseline instead settles on a conservative split that keeps energy low at a latency cost. Both operating points satisfy all three feasibility constraints ($F^1{=}F^2{=}F^3{=}0$); they are different Pareto points of the same objective, selected by the two learners' different inductive biases. Practitioners can steer Quantum-HRL toward the energy-lean end of the front simply by increasing $\beta_2$ in the reward of Eq.~\eqref{eq:reward}---the framework itself is agnostic to the chosen balance. We report both metrics transparently rather than tuning the objective post-hoc to favour our method.

\paragraph{Trade-offs and overheads.}
The benefits above are not free. Each VQC update requires $2W_{\mathrm{VQC}}$ circuit evaluations via the Parameter-Shift Rule; each BO step incurs an additional GP regression that scales cubically with the number of stored observations (a constant cost per update with a moving window). On classical simulators the wall-clock per episode is therefore comparable to, and on noisy back-ends potentially higher than, that of a plain DQN. The advantage of Quantum-HRL is qualitatively different: \emph{model size and circuit count} scale logarithmically with the problem dimension, whereas the classical baseline scales linearly in $n$ and in $h$. This positions our method as the favourable choice when scaling the network (large $M$, large $n$) or when sampling-efficient training is critical (e.g., on real NISQ hardware).

\paragraph{Scalability.}
The two scaling axes of practical interest are (i)~the state dimension $n$ and (ii)~the per-tier node count $M_l$. Axis~(i) couples only to the VQC through $q{=}\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$, so a doubling of $n$ adds a single qubit and one $R_Y$ per layer; the classical baseline, in contrast, doubles every input-layer weight. Axis~(ii) couples only to QAOA through $H_C\in\mathbb{C}^{2^{M_{l^*}}\times 2^{M_{l^*}}}$, whose preparation depth is linear in $M_{l^*}$; the QAOA parameter count remains $2p$ regardless. The framework therefore scales gracefully along both axes provided the available qubits suffice; otherwise QAOA can be run on the largest tier as a quantum solver \emph{warm-started} from a classical heuristic, leaving small tiers to direct enumeration.

\paragraph{Limitations and failure modes.}
Three regimes deserve special caution. \emph{(a)~Barren-plateau regime.} For large $L$ and shallow problem-specific structure, VQC training can suffer from exponentially vanishing gradients; our small $L{=}4$ already sits at the boundary of safe values and would require either entanglement-aware initialisation or layer-wise pre-training before scaling further \cite{mcclean2018barren}. \emph{(b)~Dense QAOA landscapes.} When the per-node costs are nearly degenerate, the QAOA approximation ratio at $p{=}2$ can drop, occasionally returning a sub-optimal but feasible node. The intrinsic reward $R_2$ smooths this through the empirical-cost feedback (Step~5 of Section~\ref{subsec:training}), but a higher $p$ should be considered in such regimes. \emph{(c)~Realistic hardware noise.} While Bayesian Optimisation absorbs i.i.d.\ Gaussian noise gracefully, correlated drifts in calibration parameters violate the GP assumptions; intermittent re-calibration of the GP prior is therefore advisable on real devices.

\paragraph{Future improvements.}
We identify several research directions that follow directly from the analysis above. (1)~\emph{Hardware deployment.} Running the full pipeline on a real superconducting or trapped-ion back-end will test whether the simulated noise model is conservative or optimistic. (2)~\emph{Hardware-efficient ansatz.} Replacing the all-to-all $R_Y$+CNOT layer with a topology-matched ansatz can reduce gate counts and improve fidelity. (3)~\emph{Multi-task concurrent offloading.} Extending the action space to handle dependencies between concurrent tasks would require either a richer VQC observable or a quantum-classical actor--critic decomposition. (4)~\emph{Federated quantum learning.} Distributing policy updates across vehicular fleets through quantum-aware federated averaging is a natural follow-up given the small parameter footprint reported here. (5)~\emph{Data re-uploading and quantum embeddings.} Augmenting amplitude encoding with re-uploading layers may further increase the expressive power of the VQC at modest qubit cost. (6)~\emph{Quantum-advantage analysis.} A formal complexity-theoretic separation between Quantum-HRL and any polynomial-time classical heuristic for the same QUBO instance would strengthen the theoretical claims.

\section{Complexity Analysis}
\label{sec:complexity}

\subsection{Classical HRL Parameter Count (Baseline)}

The classical HRL baseline of Shinde and Tarchi~\cite{hrl_ntn} deploys three fully-connected DQNs, each with $\tilde{L} = 4$ hidden layers~\cite[Sec.~IV-A]{hrl_ntn} of width $h$ neurons. Note that~\cite{hrl_ntn} does not specify the hidden width $h$; for the numerical estimates below we therefore adopt the conventional value $h{=}256$ as a representative setting and report the scaling formulae so that any other choice of $h$ can be substituted by the reader.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{P1 (tier selector)}: input $n$, output $|\mathcal{A}_1| = |\mathcal{L}| = 4$ actions.
  \item \textbf{P2 (node selector)}: input $n$, output $|\mathcal{A}_2| = \max_l M_l$ actions (up to $M_1 = 5$ for RSU).
  \item \textbf{P3 (ratio regressor)}: input $n$, output $|\mathcal{A}_3|$ discretized $\alpha$ steps.
\end{itemize}
Each DQN's trainable weight count follows:
\begin{equation}
  W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(p)} = n \cdot h
  + (\tilde{L}-2)\,h^2
  + h \cdot |\mathcal{A}_p|,
  \label{eq:dqn_params}
\end{equation}
where the first term is the input-to-first-hidden projection, the middle term is $(\tilde{L}-2)$ hidden-to-hidden projections, and the last term is the output head. Additionally, each DQN is \emph{doubled} into primary and target networks (as in standard DQN training~\cite{hrl_ntn}), so the total memory footprint is $2W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(p)}$ per agent. The three-agent pipeline yields:
\begin{equation}
  W_{\mathrm{HRL}} = 2\sum_{p=1}^{3} W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(p)}
  = O\!\left(3\,n\,h\right).
  \label{eq:hrl_params}
\end{equation}
To remain consistent with the released implementation, the head-to-head latency/energy comparison of Section~\ref{subsec:results} uses a deliberately \emph{lightweight} single-hidden-layer instantiation ($\tilde{L}{=}2$, $h{=}256$, $n{=}20$); this gives a conservative lower bound on the baseline parameter budget:
\begin{align}
  W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(1)} &= 20 \cdot 256 + 256 \cdot 4  = 6{,}144 \nonumber\\
  W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(2)} &= 20 \cdot 256 + 256 \cdot 5  = 6{,}400 \nonumber\\
  W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(3)} &= 20 \cdot 256 + 256 \cdot 10 = 7{,}680 \nonumber\\
  W_{\mathrm{HRL}} &= 6{,}144 + 6{,}400 + 7{,}680 = \mathbf{20{,}224}\ \text{(trainable)}.
  \label{eq:hrl_total}
\end{align}
This is already the figure quoted in the abstract and Table~\ref{tab:results}. The original four-hidden-layer configuration of~\cite[Sec.~IV-A]{hrl_ntn} ($\tilde{L}{=}4$) is substantially larger---each DQN then carries two additional $h^2$ projections, $W_{\mathrm{DQN}}^{(p)}\!\approx\!1.38\times10^{5}$, for $W_{\mathrm{HRL}}\!\approx\!4.1\times10^{5}$ trainable parameters (and roughly $8.2\times10^{5}$ once the primary/target copies are counted)---so the single-hidden figure understates, rather than overstates, the gap.

\subsection{Quantum-HRL Parameter Count}

\textbf{VQC} (replaces P1 + P3): Amplitude Encoding compresses the $n$-dimensional state into $q = \lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ qubits. An $L$-layer VQC with one $R_Y$ rotation per qubit per layer has:
\begin{equation}
  W_{\mathrm{VQC}} = L \cdot \lceil\log_2 n\rceil.
  \label{eq:vqc_params}
\end{equation}
There is no primary/target network duplication: VQC parameters are updated in-place via the Parameter-Shift Rule and Bayesian Optimization without a separate target copy.

\textbf{QAOA} (replaces P2): Node selection over $M_{l^*}$ nodes with circuit depth $p$ uses:
\begin{equation}
  W_{\mathrm{QAOA}} = 2p,
  \label{eq:qaoa_params}
\end{equation}
independent of $M_{l^*}$. The QUBO cost coefficients $c_n$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:qubo}) are computed deterministically from the environment state---they are not learned parameters.

\textbf{Total}: $W_{\mathrm{Q\text{-}HRL}} = L\lceil\log_2 n\rceil + 2p$. For $n{=}20$, $L{=}4$, $p{=}2$:
\begin{equation}
  W_{\mathrm{Q\text{-}HRL}} = 4 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 2 = \mathbf{24}\ \text{parameters}.
  \label{eq:qhrl_total}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reduction Factor and Interpretation}

\begin{equation}
  \rho = \frac{W_{\mathrm{HRL}}}{W_{\mathrm{Q\text{-}HRL}}}
  = \frac{20{,}224}{24} \approx \mathbf{843\times}
  \quad\text{(single-hidden-layer baseline)},
  \label{eq:reduction}
\end{equation}
rising to $\rho \approx 1.7\times10^{4}$ against the four-hidden-layer baseline configuration of~\cite{hrl_ntn} ($W_{\mathrm{HRL}}\approx4.1\times10^{5}$).
This reduction is not merely a constant: it reflects a \emph{qualitative change in scaling}. Classical DQN parameter count grows as $O(n \cdot h)$ in both state dimension and hidden width. VQC parameter count grows as $O(\log_2 n)$ in state dimension, independent of any analogue of $h$. QAOA parameter count is $O(p)$, independent of the number of nodes $M_{l^*}$.

The practical consequence is threefold. First, the number of quantum circuit evaluations per BO update is $O(1)$ per parameter vs.\ $O(k \cdot n \cdot h)$ gradient computations per DQN batch. Second, the absence of a target network copy halves memory. Third, the QAOA node-selector requires no stored Q-value table: the cost Hamiltonian $H_C$ is recomputed from the current environment state at each step, making the low-level policy \emph{stateless}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \textbf{Quantum-HRL}, a hybrid quantum--classical hierarchical reinforcement learning framework for adaptive task offloading in multi-layer T-NTN vehicular edge computing. By replacing the classical tri-DQN pipeline of~\cite{hrl_ntn} with a VQC-driven high-level policy and a QAOA-based combinatorial node selector tuned through Bayesian Optimisation~\cite{bo_qaoa}, the framework reduces the trainable-parameter count from $O(n\cdot h)$ to $O(L\log_2 n + p)$---$24$ parameters versus $20{,}224$ for the baseline, an $843\times$ compression. On the five-seed T-NTN benchmark, Quantum-HRL delivers $20.3\%$ lower mean task latency than the classical HRL baseline ($0.117\pm0.038$ vs.\ $0.147\pm0.016$\,s; $p<10^{-3}$ under both Welch's $t$ and Mann--Whitney $U$ tests) with zero deadline, sojourn, or energy-budget violations, at the cost of higher per-task energy ($0.97$ vs.\ $0.35$\,J)---a transparent latency--energy trade-off whose operating point is steerable through the reward weights. Ablation confirms that both quantum modules contribute: removing QAOA doubles latency, and removing the VQC degrades it by more than an order of magnitude. The contribution rests on three structural pillars: (i)~Amplitude Encoding provides a logarithmic-in-$n$ qubit requirement and a correspondingly compact state representation~\cite{biamonte2017qml}; (ii)~QAOA exploits the algebraic structure of the node-selection QUBO~\cite{farhi2014quantum}; and (iii)~Bayesian Optimisation furnishes a noise-aware outer loop that is compatible with current NISQ hardware~\cite{preskill2018nisq,bo_qaoa}.

Future research will explore (a) end-to-end deployment of Quantum-HRL on real superconducting and trapped-ion back-ends, (b) hardware-efficient ans\"atze and data re-uploading layers that mitigate barren-plateau effects, (c) multi-task concurrent offloading with quantum actor--critic decomposition, and (d) federated quantum learning across vehicular fleets that exploits the small parameter footprint of the proposed framework. We also see value in deriving formal quantum--classical separation results for the QUBO instances arising from T-NTN offloading, thereby placing the empirical advantages on a stronger theoretical footing.
