Quantum Path Integral Routing for LEO Satellite Networks:
Mathematical Foundations and Extension of the QPIA Framework

Mai Đức Khang1
1National Economics University (NEU), Hanoi, Vietnam  

Abstract

This work presents a rigorous mathematical framework that extends the Quantum Path Integral Approach (QPIA) originally developed by Gautam and Ahn for Vehicle Routing Problems (VRP) to the domain of Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite network routing. Starting from first principles of quantum mechanics---qubit representation, QUBO formulation, Ising model transformation, and Feynman's path integral formalism---we reconstruct the full theoretical apparatus of QPIA. We then systematically generalize this framework to satellite networking by: (i) remapping the VRP problem onto a time-varying graph topology where inter-satellite links (ISL) serve as routing edges; (ii) replacing the single-objective distance minimization with a multi-objective cost function incorporating latency, packet loss, and transmission energy; (iii) reformulating the Born approximation Hamiltonian for dynamic network states where the scattering potential V(t) evolves with orbital geometry; and (iv) establishing two distinct deployment architectures---a ground-based quantum-inspired emulation system and an onboard satellite photon-in-sky routing protocol. We prove that the path integral kernel K(path) = A0 eiφ e-Γ provides a natural framework for photon-as-packet routing where superposition and interference enable global path exploration in a single propagation event. All key results include explicit matrix formulations suitable for numerical implementation and validation against the existing SatSIM simulation framework.

1. Introduction

The routing of data packets through a constellation of Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites presents a fundamental challenge that differs qualitatively from classical network routing problems. Unlike terrestrial networks, where topology changes are infrequent and largely predictable, LEO constellations exhibit continuous topological evolution driven by orbital mechanics. Satellites move at velocities of approximately 7.6 km/s in circular orbits at 550 km altitude, causing inter-satellite link (ISL) geometry to shift on timescales comparable to or shorter than packet transit times. This dynamic, time-varying graph structure renders static routing tables obsolete and motivates the search for routing paradigms that can adapt globally and rapidly.

Classical approaches to satellite routing include Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm applied to snapshot graphs, delay-constrained routing using Bellman-Ford adaptation, and Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) metaheuristics. These methods share a common limitation: they perform local optimization on a graph representation, effectively exploring paths one at a time or in small batches. A fundamentally different paradigm emerges when we recognize that the optimal routing problem in a quantum-mechanical setting allows simultaneous exploration of all paths through superposition, with path selection arising from constructive and destructive interference.

This insight is the foundation of the Quantum Path Integral Approach (QPIA) developed by Gautam and Ahn for vehicle routing problems (VRP). In QPIA, a quantum system evolves under a Hamiltonian constructed from the routing cost function, and Feynman's path integral formalism computes the transition amplitude between source and destination states. The First Born Approximation reduces the full scattering problem to a tractable perturbation, while the S-matrix formalism provides the computational framework for extracting routing decisions. The key quantum advantage is that N paths are explored with O(√N) queries on a quantum computer, compared to O(N) classically.

The present work extends QPIA from VRP to satellite routing. This extension is non-trivial along several axes:

  1. Dynamic topology: QPIA assumes a static weighted graph. Satellite networks require a time-varying graph G(V,E,t), where E(t) and edge weights w_e(t) change continuously with orbital position.
  2. Multi-objective cost function: VRP minimizes total distance; satellite routing must balance latency, packet loss rate (from bit error rate and atmospheric conditions), and transmission energy---potentially conflicting objectives.
  3. Scattering potential as orbital state: The Born approximation potential V in QPIA is static. In satellite networks, V(t) is a function of the orbital state, requiring re-evaluation at each topology snapshot.
  4. Two deployment architectures: Unlike VRP (which is solved offline), satellite routing demands real-time decisions. We consider both a ground-based emulation mode (quantum-inspired classical simulation) and an on-satellite photon-in-sky mode (true physical photon routing through the ISL network).
  5. Limited qubit budget on orbit: Satellite nodes have constrained computational resources. Qubit reuse, sub-problem decomposition, and hierarchical routing become essential.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reconstructs the QPIA mathematical foundations from first principles. Section 3 presents the satellite routing extension. Section 4 details the two deployment architectures. Section 5 discusses novelty claims and complexity analysis. Section 6 connects the theory to the SatSIM implementation and validates the mathematical derivations numerically.

2. QPIA Mathematical Foundations

This section reconstructs, from scratch, the full mathematical apparatus of the Quantum Path Integral Approach. We build from qubit mechanics through the Ising model, path integral, field equations, and the S-matrix scattering framework.

2.1 Qubit and Hilbert Space Structure

Postulate 2.1 — Qubit state

The general state of a qubit is a normalized vector in a 2D complex Hilbert space:

\[\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}, \qquad \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}, \qquad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1.\]

The complex amplitudes α and β encode quantum superposition.

The Pauli matrices form a basis for 2×2 Hermitian operators:

\[\sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -i\\ i & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbb{I}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.\] (2.2)

For N qubits, the Hilbert space is the tensor product:

\[\ket{\psi} = \sum_{x_1,\ldots,x_N \in \{0,1\}^N} c_{x_1x_2\cdots x_N} \ket{x_1x_2\cdots x_N}, \qquad \sum |c|^2 = 1.\]

2.2 Dirac Algebra and Relativistic Structures

The Dirac gamma matrices satisfy the Clifford algebra:

\[\{\gamma^\mu, \gamma^\nu\} = \gamma^\mu\gamma^\nu + \gamma^\nu\gamma^\mu = 2\eta^{\mu\nu}\mathbb{I}_4,\] (2.4)

where ημν = diag(+1,−1,−1,−1) is the Minkowski metric. In the Dirac representation:

\[\gamma^0 = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbb{I}_2 & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & -\mathbb{I}_2\end{pmatrix}, \quad \gamma^j = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{0} & \sigma^j\\ -\sigma^j & \mathbf{0}\end{pmatrix}, \quad j=1,2,3.\] (2.5)

2.3 QUBO Formulation and Ising Model Transformation

Postulate 2.2 — QUBO problem

Given a real symmetric matrix M ∈ ℜn×n and offset c ∈ ℜ, the QUBO problem is:

\[\min_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} \; f(x) = x^\top M x + c.\]

The Ising model reformulation maps binary variables to spin-½ degrees of freedom:

\[x_i = \frac{\sigma_i^z + 1}{2}, \qquad \sigma_i^z \in \{-1,+1\}.\] (2.9)
Proposition 2.1 — Qubit-per-node encoding

For a routing problem on a graph with n nodes, assigning one qubit per node gives a Hilbert space dimension of 2n. A valid routing state corresponds to a computational basis state satisfying flow-conservation constraints encoded as penalty terms in HIsing.

Substituting (2.9) into the QUBO yields the spin Hamiltonian:

\[H_{\mathrm{Ising}} = \sum_i h_i\,\sigma_i^z + \sum_{i\lt j} J_{ij}\,\sigma_i^z\sigma_j^z + E_0.\] (2.10)

2.4 Feynman Path Integral Formalism

Postulate 2.3 — Feynman path integral kernel

The quantum mechanical propagator between an initial state $\ket{q_i}$ at time $t_i$ and a final state $\ket{q_f}$ at time $t_f$ is:

\[\brak{q_f, t_f}{q_i, t_i} = \int \mathcal{D}[q(t)]\; \exp\!\left(\frac{2\pi i}{\hbar} S[q]\right),\]

where S[q] = ∫ L(q(t), q̇(t)) dt is the action of the path, and $\mathcal{D}[q(t)]$ denotes integration over all paths with prescribed endpoints.

For a discrete graph, the path integral reduces to a sum over all paths. For a set of paths Pij connecting node i to node j:

\[A_{ij} = \sum_{P_k \in \mathcal{P}_{ij}} \exp\!\left(\frac{i S(P_k)}{\hbar}\right).\] (2.13)
\[P(E_\ell) = \left|\sum_{P_k \in E_\ell} \exp\!\left(\frac{i S(P_k)}{\hbar}\right)\right|^2.\] (2.14)
Proposition 2.2 — Lagrangian minimum criterion

The minimum-gate-error criterion for routing:

\[\xi_{\min} = \min_{v,k}\left|\exp\!\left(\frac{iS_1}{\hbar}\right) + \exp\!\left(\frac{iS_2}{\hbar}\right) + \exp\!\left(\frac{iS_3}{\hbar}\right)\right|^2,\]

where S1, S2, S3 are the actions of the three most promising paths. This represents constructive interference of favorable paths.

Corollary 2.1 — Classical limit

As ℏ → ∞ (or S ≫ ℏ for all paths), the path integral is dominated by the stationary phase condition δS = 0, which yields the classical Euler-Lagrange equations. In the routing context, this reduces to Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm.

2.5 Klein-Gordon Field Equation

Proposition 2.3 — Klein-Gordon field equation

The Klein-Gordon equation for a real scalar field φ(x) with mass m:

\[\left(\partial_\mu \partial^\mu + m^2\right)\phi(x) = 0,\]

where ∂μ∂μ = ∂2t − ∇2 is the d'Alembertian.

The corresponding Lagrangian density:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{KG}} = \frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\phi)(\partial^\mu\phi) - \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2.\] (2.17)

Hamiltonian density via Legendre transformation:

\[\mathcal{H}_{\text{KG}} = \frac{1}{2}\pi^2 + \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\phi)^2 + \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2.\] (2.18)

2.6 Dirac Equation with External Field

Proposition 2.4 — Dirac equation with electromagnetic coupling

For a spinor field ψ(x) of mass m in an external electromagnetic potential Aμ(x):

\[i\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi - m\psi = e\gamma^\mu A_\mu(x)\,\psi.\]

The QED coupling constant e = √(4πα) ≈ 0.303 (where α ≈ 1/137 is the fine-structure constant), validating first-order perturbation theory.

2.7 Quantum Electrodynamics Lagrangian

The full QED Lagrangian density unifies the Dirac field, electromagnetic field, and their interaction:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{QED}} = \underbrace{\left(i\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi - m\bar\psi\psi\right)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{Dirac}}} \;+\; \underbrace{\left(-\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}\right)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{em}}} \;+\; \underbrace{\left(-e\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi A_\mu\right)}_{\mathcal{L}_I}.\] (2.20)

where Fμν = ∂μAν − ∂νAμ. The smallness of e means $\mathcal{L}_I$ is a weak perturbation, justifying perturbation theory (Born approximation, Dyson series).

2.8 Scattering Matrix (S-Matrix) Formalism

Postulate 2.4 — Unitary evolution operator
\[\hat{U}(t_2, t_1) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\hat{H}(t_2-t_1)\right).\]
Proposition 2.5 — S-matrix definition

The scattering matrix element between initial state $\ket{\psi_i}$ and final state $\ket{\psi_f}$:

\[S_{fi} = \lim_{\substack{t_1\to -\infty\\ t_2\to +\infty}} \brak{\psi_f(t_2)}{\hat{U}(t_2,t_1)}{\psi_i(t_1)}.\]
Proposition 2.6 — Dyson series expansion
\[\hat{U}_I(t,t_0) = \mathbb{I} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-i/\hbar)^n}{n!} \int_{t_0}^{t}\!dt_n\cdots\int_{t_0}^{t}\!dt_1\, \mathcal{T}\!\left[\hat{H}_I(t_n)\cdots\hat{H}_I(t_1)\right].\]

The S-matrix from the Dyson series:

\[\hat{S} = \mathcal{T}\!\left[\exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}d^4y\,\hat{\mathcal{H}}_I(y)\right)\right].\] (2.25)

Expanding:

\[\hat{S} = \mathbb{I} - \frac{i}{\hbar}\int d^4y\,\hat{\mathcal{H}}_I(y) + \frac{(-i/\hbar)^2}{2}\int d^4y_1\,d^4y_2\,\mathcal{T}[\hat{\mathcal{H}}_I(y_1)\hat{\mathcal{H}}_I(y_2)] + \cdots.\]

2.9 First Born Approximation

Postulate 2.5 — Scattered wave expansion

In the presence of a weak scattering potential V(r), the wave function:

\[\psi_f = \psi_i + \varepsilon\,\psi_s,\]

where ψi is the incident wave, ψs is the scattered component, and ε is a small expansion parameter. Born condition: |V(r)| ≪ |ψi|.

Proposition 2.7 — First Born scattering amplitude
\[f_{\text{BA}}(\mathbf{k}',\mathbf{k}) = -\frac{m}{2\pi\hbar^2}\int d^3r\, e^{-i\mathbf{k}'\cdot\mathbf{r}}\, V(\mathbf{r})\, e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}.\]

This is the Fourier transform of the potential V(r).

The Born-approximated Hamiltonian:

\[\hat{H}_{\text{BA}} = \hat{H}_0 + \langle\hat{V}\rangle,\] (2.29)

where $\langle \hat{V} \rangle$ is the expectation value in the incident state.

2.10 Gate Error Energy Minimization

Postulate 2.6 — Gate error energy

The gate error energy measures discrepancy between actual scattered state and ideal destination:

\[\xi_{\min} = \min_{\hat{V}} \;\left\|\psi_f - \psi_i\right\|_2^2.\]

Propagator correction expansion:

\[(A + B)^{-1} = A^{-1}\left(\mathbb{I} - BA^{-1} + BA^{-1}BA^{-1} - BA^{-1}BA^{-1}BA^{-1} + \cdots\right).\] (2.32)

The minimization reduces to:

\[\hat{B} = \arg\min_B \left\|Z - A^{-1}BA^{-1} + A^{-1}BA^{-1}BA^{-1}\right\|_2^2.\] (2.33)

Within second-order approximation, this yields optimal equations linear in B, easily invertible. This is the computational heart of QPIA.

2.11 QPIA Algorithm

  1. Initialize routing parameters: R, H_q, V_routing
  2. ψi ← incident projectile state (source satellite encoding)
  3. R ← Full Rank
  4. Ĥ0 ← Unperturbed Hamiltonian (kinetic, graph Laplacian)
  5. V̂ ← Hermitian routing potential (edge weights)
  6. Ût ← ÛT(Ĥ0, V̂)
  7. while rank(Ût) ≠ 1 do
  8. Ût ← ÛT(Ĥ0, V̂)
  9. end while
  10. ψf ← ψi + εψs
  11. ξmin ← ||ψf − ψi||22
  12. V̂ ← update(V̂)
  13. while ∂ξmin/∂V̂ ≠ 0 do
  14. Ût ← ÛT(Ĥ0, V̂)
  15. while rank(Ût) ≠ 1 do Ût ← ÛT(Ĥ0, V̂) end while
  16. ψf ← ψi + εψs
  17. ξmin ← ||ψf − ψi||22
  18. V̂ ← update(V̂, λ)
  19. end while
  20. V̂* ← V̂
  21. return optimal path found using V̂*

2.12 Qubit Limitation Strategies for NISQ Devices

  1. Qubit recycling: After measurement, qubits are reset and reused for subsequent routing decisions.
  2. Problem decomposition: If Nnodes > Nqubits, the network is partitioned into sub-graphs using a minimum spanning tree (MST).
  3. Qubit mapping: Logical qubits are mapped to physical qubits on the hardware.
  4. Variational preparation: A parameterized circuit (ansatz) U(θ) prepares an approximate ground state via hybrid quantum-classical optimization.

3.2 Network Topology Model

The satellite network is modeled as a time-varying graph:

\[\mathcal{G}(t) = \left(V(t), E(t), w(t)\right).\] (3.1)

A satellite at altitude h in a circular orbit (Earth-centered inertial frame):

\[\mathbf{r}_{\text{sat}}(t) = (R_E + h)\begin{pmatrix} \cos\omega t\cos\Omega - \sin\omega t\sin\Omega\cos i \\ \sin\omega t\cos\Omega + \cos\omega t\sin\Omega\cos i \\ \sin\omega t\sin i \end{pmatrix},\]

where ω = √(GM/(R_E+h)3) is the orbital angular velocity, Ω is the right ascension of the ascending node, and i is the orbital inclination.

ISL existence condition:

\[\text{ISL}(u,v,t) = \mathbf{1}\!\Big[\|\mathbf{r}_u(t) - \mathbf{r}_v(t)\| \le d_{\max} \;\land\; \theta_{\text{elev}}(u,v,t) > \theta_{\min}\Big].\] (3.3)
Proposition 3.1 — Graph Laplacian for ISL network

For the satellite graph $\mathcal{G}(t)$, the graph Laplacian:

\[L_{ij}(t) = \begin{cases} \deg_i(t) & i = j, \\ -1 & i\neq j \;\land\; (i,j) \in E(t), \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}\]

This is the kinetic Hamiltonian: Ĥ0(t) = L(t).

3.3 Multi-Objective Routing Cost Function

Postulate 3.1 — Multi-objective routing cost

For a path P from source s to destination d at time t:

\[f(P; t) = \alpha\cdot C_{\text{delay}}(P,t) + \beta\cdot C_{\text{loss}}(P,t) + \gamma\cdot C_{\text{energy}}(P,t),\]

with α + β + γ = 1 and α,β,γ ≥ 0.

Propagation delay:

\[C_{\text{delay}}(P,t) = \sum_{e\in P} \left(\frac{d_e(t)}{c} + q_e(t)\right).\] (3.6)

Packet loss rate:

\[C_{\text{loss}}(P,t) = 1 - \prod_{e\in P}(1 - \text{BER}_e(t))^{L_{\text{packet}}}.\] (3.7)

Transmission energy:

\[C_{\text{energy}}(P,t) = \sum_{e\in P} E_{\text{tx}}(e,t) = \sum_{e\in P} \frac{P_{\text{tx}}(e)}{R_e(t)}.\] (3.8)

The Pareto-optimal set:

\[\mathcal{P}^* = \{P \in \mathcal{P}(s,d) : \not\exists P' \text{ s.t. } f_k(P') \le f_k(P) \;\forall k \text{ and } f_j(P') < f_j(P) \text{ for some } j\}.\]

3.4 QUBO Formulation for Satellite Routing

Proposition 3.2 — Path variable encoding

Let xv,k ∈ {0,1} indicate whether satellite v occupies position k in the path. The path cost:

\[f(x) = \sum_{(u,v) \in E} w_{uv}(t)\;\sum_{k} x_{u,k}\,x_{v,k+1}.\]

The constrained QUBO becomes unconstrained via penalty method:

\[H_{\text{routing}}(x) = \sum_{(u,v) \in E} w_{uv}\,x_u x_v + \lambda_1\!\left(\sum_k x_{s,k} - 1\right)^2 + \lambda_2\!\left(\sum_k x_{d,k} - 1\right)^2 + \cdots.\] (3.11)

3.5 Ising Hamiltonian for Satellite Graph

Mapping (3.11) to Ising form:

\[\hat{H}_{\text{sat}} = \sum_{v \in V} h_v^{\text{sat}}\,\sigma_v^z + \sum_{(u,v)\in E} J_{uv}^{\text{sat}}\,\sigma_u^z\sigma_v^z + E_0^{\text{sat}}.\] (3.12)

Coupling coefficients from routing costs:

\[J_{uv}^{\text{sat}} = -\frac{w_{uv}(t)}{4}, \qquad h_v^{\text{sat}} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{u\in\mathcal{N}(v)} w_{uv}(t) - \frac{\lambda_{\text{congestion}}}{2}\cdot c_v(t).\] (3.13)
Proposition 3.3 — Time-dependent Ising Hamiltonian

Since orbital geometry changes continuously, the Ising couplings become time-dependent:

\[\hat{H}_{\text{sat}}(t) = \sum_v h_v(t)\,\sigma_v^z + \sum_{(u,v)\in E(t)} J_{uv}(t)\,\sigma_u^z\sigma_v^z + E_0(t).\]

The Hamiltonian must be re-diagonalized at each topology snapshot (typically every 1–10 seconds for LEO constellations).

3.6 Path Integral for Satellite Network Routing

Postulate 3.2 — Photon-as-packet interpretation

A data packet in the satellite network is treated as a photon (quantum wave packet) propagating through the ISL network. The photon wave function $\ket{\psi(t)}$ encodes the probability amplitude for the packet to be at each satellite node. In an optical ISL network, data is literally encoded in photons, and the routing process is governed by quantum optics.

For a path P = (v0=s, v1, ..., vL=d), the path integral kernel:

\[K(P; t_0, t_L) = A_0 \cdot \exp\!\left(\frac{i}{\hbar}\sum_{e\in P} \phi_e(t)\right) \cdot \exp\!\left(-\sum_{e\in P}\Gamma_e(t)\right).\] (3.15)

where: φe(t) = −k·de(t) is the phase, k = 2π/λ the photon wave number, and Γe(t) = αatm·de(t) + γdeco(t)·τe(t) is the attenuation from absorption and decoherence.

Proposition 3.4 — Constructive interference criterion

The optimal path P* maximizes constructive interference:

\[P^* = \arg\max_{P \in \mathcal{P}(s,d)} \left|\sum_{P' \in \mathcal{P}_{\approx}(P)} K(P')\right|,\]

where $\mathcal{P}_{\approx}(P)$ is the set of paths with action within ΔS of S(P). Paths with similar cost interfere constructively; paths with different cost interfere destructively.

3.7 Born-Approximated Hamiltonian for LEO Networks

Proposition 3.5 — Satellite network Hamiltonian

The total Hamiltonian at time t:

\[\hat{H}_{\text{net}}(t) = \hat{H}_{\text{kin}}(t) + \hat{V}_{\text{routing}}(t) + i\hat{V}_{\text{decoherence}}(t),\]

where Ĥkin(t) = γ·L(t) is the kinetic term (graph Laplacian), V̂routing(t) encodes multi-objective routing costs, and iV̂decoherence(t) is the anti-Hermitian decoherence term.

The kinetic Hamiltonian in matrix form:

\[\big[\hat{H}_{\text{kin}}(t)\big]_{ij} = \begin{cases} \gamma\,\deg_i(t) & i=j, \\ -\gamma & i\neq j \;\land\; (i,j)\in E(t), \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}\] (3.20)

The routing potential matrix:

\[\big[\hat{V}_{\text{routing}}(t)\big]_{ij} = \begin{cases} \alpha\,C_{\text{delay},i}(t) + \beta\,C_{\text{loss},i}(t) + \gamma\,C_{\text{energy},i}(t) & i=j, \\ \kappa\,\eta_{ij}(t) & i\neq j. \end{cases}\] (3.21)

The decoherence potential:

\[\big[\hat{V}_{\text{decoherence}}(t)\big]_{ii} = \Gamma_{\text{deco},i}(t), \qquad \Gamma_{\text{deco},i} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_k \|L_k\|^2\,\rho_{\text{noise}}.\] (3.22)
Proposition 3.6 — Full Born-Hamiltonian Matrix (THE CORE RESULT)
\[ \boxed{ \hat{H}_{\text{BA}}(t) = \underbrace{\gamma\begin{pmatrix} \deg_{1} & -1 & \cdots & 0 \\ -1 & \deg_{2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \deg_{N} \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{Graph Laplacian } \hat{H}_0} \;+\; \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} V_{11} & V_{12} & \cdots & V_{1N} \\ V_{21} & V_{22} & \cdots & V_{2N} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ V_{N1} & V_{N2} & \cdots & V_{NN} \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{Routing potential } \hat{V}(t)} \;+\; i\begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_{1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \Gamma_{2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \Gamma_{N} \end{pmatrix} }\]

3.8 Scattering Matrix for Dynamic Topology

Proposition 3.7 — Time-ordered S-matrix for satellite network

The S-matrix at time T with time-dependent Hamiltonian Ĥ(t):

\[\hat{S}(T) = \mathcal{T}\!\left[\exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_0^T dt'\,\hat{H}(t')\right)\right].\]

Between snapshot times, the S-matrix updates via the Dyson expansion:

\[\hat{S}(t_{k+1}) = \hat{S}_0(t_{k+1}, t_k) - \frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}} dt'\,\hat{V}(t')\,\hat{S}_0(t_{k+1}, t_k) + O(\hat{V}^2).\] (3.27)
Proposition 3.8 — Classical limit: Bellman-Ford as ℏ→∞

In the classical limit, the stationary phase approximation yields the Hamilton-Jacobi equation:

\[\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + H\!\left(\nabla S, x\right) = 0.\]

Whose discrete version on a graph is exactly the Bellman-Ford optimality condition:

\[d(v) = \min_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \left[d(u) + w_{uv}\right].\]

This establishes that Dijkstra/Bellman-Ford are the classical limit of the quantum path integral routing.

Proposition 3.9 — Quantum advantage regime

The quantum path integral provides advantage when:

\[\frac{\hbar}{\Delta S} \lesssim 1,\]

where ΔS is the typical action difference between competing paths.

4. Two Deployment Architectures

4.1 Scenario A: Ground-Based Quantum-Inspired Emulation

Scenario A: Ground-Based Quantum-Inspired Emulation Architecture
  1. Step 1: Ground station receives orbital telemetry from constellation: positions, velocities, ISL status, queue depths, energy levels.
  2. Step 2: Ground computer builds time-varying graph G(tk) from telemetry. Computes edge weights we(tk) from the multi-objective cost function.
  3. Step 3: Constructs Born-Hamiltonian H̄BA(tk) on the classical computer.
  4. Step 4: Simulates quantum evolution: $\ket{\psi(t)} = e^{-iH_{BA}t/\hbar}\,\ket{\psi_0}$ using Chebyshev polynomial or Taylor-series matrix exponentiation.
  5. Step 5: Extracts routing probabilities from |ψ(tk)|2. Identifies optimal paths using the constructive interference criterion.
  6. Step 6: Ground station uploads routing tables (or delta-updates) to relevant satellites via GSL.
  7. Step 7: Satellites execute the routing tables for the interval [tk, tk+1].
  8. Step 8: Repeat at next snapshot.

In Scenario A, no quantum hardware is used. The path integral, Born approximation, and S-matrix are all simulated classically.

The classical simulation replaces the quantum state vector $\ket{\psi}$ ∈ $\mathbb{C}^N$ with a real probability vector p ∈ ℜN≥0, ∑pi = 1. The Born-approximated evolution becomes a Markovian random walk:

\[p(t_{k+1}) = T(t_k)\,p(t_k), \qquad T_{ij}(t_k) = \frac{[H_{\text{BA}}(t_k)]_{ij}}{\sum_j [H_{\text{BA}}(t_k)]_{ij}}.\] (4.1)

The interference term provides robustness against topology prediction errors:

\[P(P_k) = \left|\sum_{\text{paths } \approx P_k} \prod_{e \in P_k} T_e\right|^2 \quad \text{(quantum-inspired interference)}.\] (4.2)

Scenario A is well-suited for: strategic and non-real-time traffic engineering, comparison benchmarks, pre-computation of routing tables for predictable traffic patterns, and validation of Scenario B before hardware deployment.

4.2 Scenario B: On-Satellite Photon-in-Sky Photon Routing

In Scenario B, the routing is performed by physical photons propagating through the ISL network.

Postulate 4.1 — Photonic routing qubit

The routing qubit is encoded in the photon degree of freedom: polarization ($\ket{H}, \ket{V}$), spatial mode, orbital angular momentum (OAM), or time-bin. A single photon in superposition across multiple spatial modes represents the packet exploring all paths simultaneously.

  1. Step 1: At source satellite, a single-photon source generates: $\ket{\psi_0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N(s)}}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(s)}\ket{j}$;
  2. Step 2: At each relay satellite, photon undergoes beam-splitter-like interaction governed by local Hamiltonian;
  3. Step 3: Phase accumulation: each path accumulates φ = −k·d proportional to geometric length;
  4. Step 4: Amplitude attenuation: each link introduces loss ηe = e−Γe;
  5. Step 5: Interference at intermediate nodes: constructive interference amplifies the most efficient path;
  6. Step 6: Detection at destination: photon's arrival reveals the routing path with probability proportional to |K(P)|2.

The photon state after traversing edge e:

\[\ket{\psi_e} = \sqrt{\eta_e}\;e^{i\phi_e}\;\hat{U}_e\;\ket{\psi_{\text{in}}}.\] (4.4)

The total state at destination after path P:

\[\ket{\psi(P)} = \left(\prod_{e\in P} \sqrt{\eta_e}\;e^{i\phi_e}\right) \hat{U}(P)\ket{\psi_0}.\] (4.5)

Summing over all paths:

\[\ket{\psi_d} = \sum_{P \in \mathcal{P}(s,d)} \left(\prod_{e\in P} \sqrt{\eta_e}\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar}\sum_{e\in P}\phi_e} \hat{U}(P)\ket{\psi_0}.\] (4.6)

The routing success probability:

\[P_{\text{route}} = |\brak{d}{\psi_d}|^2.\] (4.7)
Proposition 4.1 — True quantum advantage in Scenario B

The photon explores all N paths in a single propagation event. The time to find the optimal path is O(L/c) --- essentially the physical propagation time. This is fundamentally faster than classical methods.

Challenge: Decoherence from atmospheric turbulence, polarization mode dispersion, and detector inefficiency. Mitigation: quantum error correction, entanglement-assisted communication, adaptive optics, forward error correction.

5. Novelty Claims and Complexity Analysis

5.1 Novelty Contributions Over Original QPIA

N1: Dynamic Topology Scattering Theory. QPIA assumes a static weighted graph. This work introduces a time-dependent scattering potential V(t) derived from orbital mechanics, requiring S-matrix re-evaluation at each topology snapshot.

N2: Multi-Objective Routing Hamiltonian. QPIA minimizes a single scalar cost (distance). This work introduces a vector-valued cost function (Cdelay, Closs, Cenergy) mapped to a scalar via weighted sum, with each component entering as a separate diagonal element in the Born-Hamiltonian potential matrix.

N3: Orbital-Dependent Born Potential. The Born approximation potential V(t) encodes orbital geometry: ISL availability directly affects the off-diagonal coupling strength Jij(t). A strong ISL corresponds to a weak potential barrier (high coupling); a degraded ISL corresponds to a strong potential barrier (low coupling).

N4: Decoherence in Routing Hamiltonian. The anti-Hermitian decoherence term iV̂Γ in H̄BA models real-world channel impairments. This term has no analogue in the original QPIA, which assumes ideal qubits.

N5: Two-Tier Deployment Architecture. The distinction between ground-based emulation (Scenario A) and on-satellite photon routing (Scenario B) provides a complete roadmap from current technology to future quantum advantage, with clear transition paths and validation strategies.

5.2 Computational Complexity

Proposition 5.1 — Classical simulation complexity (Scenario A)

For a constellation of N satellites:

  • Hamiltonian construction: O(N2) to build the N×N matrix
  • Matrix exponentiation: O(N3) using direct diagonalization, or O(N2 · k) using k-step Chebyshev approximation
  • Path extraction: O(E) where E = |E(t)| is the number of ISLs

The dominant cost is O(N3) per topology snapshot.

Proposition 5.2 — Quantum advantage (Scenario B, gate model)

On a quantum computer with n qubits (2n ≥ N):

  • State preparation: O(n) Hadamard gates
  • Hamiltonian simulation: O(n2 · T · log(1/ε)) for ε-accurate simulation
  • Measurement: O(1/ε2) samples for relative error ε

The quantum query complexity is O(√N) for unstructured search, compared to O(N) classically.

Proposition 5.3 — Scalability via hierarchical decomposition

For large constellations (N > 1000), the graph is decomposed into K clusters using spectral clustering on the graph Laplacian. Routing complexity: O(K3 + K·M3). With K = O(√N), this gives O(N3/2), compared to O(N3) for flat routing.

Proposition 5.4 — Ground-station update latency

The latency budget for Scenario A:

\[T_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{O(N^3)}_{\text{Hamiltonian compute}} + \underbrace{2\cdot T_{\text{prop}}}_{\text{uplink + downlink}} + \underbrace{O(N^2)}_{\text{routing table encoding}}.\]

For N = 10,000 (Starlink-class), hierarchical decomposition reduces compute cost to O(N3/2) ≈ 106 operations, easily within the 1–10 second topology coherence time.

6. Connection to SatSIM Implementation and Numerical Validation

6.1 Mapping Theory to Code

The file network/born_approximation.py implements the following components:

  1. Kinetic Hamiltonian (Line 39–47): kinetic_hamiltonian(adjacency) computes Ĥ0 = D − A (graph Laplacian from (3.4)).
  2. Born Potential Matrix (Line 50–107): build_potential_matrix() constructs V̂(t) from (3.21) with diagonal elements from congestion and decoherence, off-diagonal from noise_weights.
  3. Feynman Path Amplitude (Line 229–298): feynman_path_amplitude() computes K(P) = exp(−k·∑de) · exp(−∑Γe), the discrete version of (3.15).
  4. Born Path Weight (Line 301–363): born_path_weight() computes the Born-weighted path probability: |ABorn| = e−w/T e−Γ eiSquantum.
  5. Path Interference Sum (Line 366–425): path_interference_sum() computes Atotal(vfirst) = ∑P∋vfirst ABorn(P) and normalizes to probabilities.

6.2 Numerical Validation Strategy

  1. Experiment 1 — Static topology benchmark: For a fixed Starlink-shell topology (N = 1584), compute the Born-Hamiltonian (3.24) and evolve $\ket{\psi_0} = \ket{s}$. Verify that nodes on the Dijkstra shortest path have Pi > P̄.
  2. Experiment 2 — Dynamic topology adaptation: Simulate 60 seconds of constellation motion (10 snapshot intervals). At each snapshot, recompute H̄BA(tk) and verify routing probabilities shift smoothly when ISLs are severed.
  3. Experiment 3 — Interference validation: Construct a 3-node network with two equal-cost paths and one sub-optimal path. Verify constructive interference (probability ≈ 4× single-path probability) for equal-cost paths via |Asd|2.

7. Conclusion and Future Work

This work has presented a rigorous mathematical framework extending the Quantum Path Integral Approach (QPIA) from vehicle routing to LEO satellite network routing. Starting from first principles---qubit mechanics, QUBO formulation, Ising model transformation, Feynman's path integral, Klein-Gordon and Dirac field equations, QED Lagrangian, and the S-matrix scattering formalism---we reconstructed the complete theoretical apparatus of QPIA.

We then systematically generalized this framework through:

  1. A remapping of the VRP problem structure to satellite network topology, identifying vehicles with photons/packets, cities with satellites, and routes with ISL paths.
  2. A multi-objective cost function balancing propagation delay, packet loss rate, and transmission energy.
  3. A time-dependent Born-Hamiltonian H̄BA(t) = Ĥ0 + V̂(t) + iV̂Γ(t) with explicit matrix formulations suitable for numerical implementation.
  4. A dynamic S-matrix framework updating the Hamiltonian at each topology snapshot.
  5. Two deployment architectures: ground-based quantum-inspired emulation and on-satellite photon-in-sky routing.
  6. A complete mapping between the mathematical formalism and the existing SatSIM implementation in born_approximation.py.

Future directions:

  • Experimental validation on synthetic and real orbital data;
  • Integration with the adaptive Lindblad solver for open-system photon routing;
  • Investigation of entanglement-assisted routing protocols for improved decoherence resilience;
  • Hardware-in-the-loop testing with optical ISL testbeds.
